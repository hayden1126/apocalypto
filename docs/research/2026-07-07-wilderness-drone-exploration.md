# Wilderness (coast/island) & Drone-Tier Exploration

_Date: 2026-07-07. Second companion to the main research report. Assesses (W) whether the app usefully degrades for a coast/island wilderness scenario, and (D) an OPTIONAL consumer-drone gear tier (aerial re-survey, route scouting, comms relay). Urban/populated disaster remains the design center._

**Method.** 9 sub-questions researched with live web search (Sonnet), 14 load-bearing quantitative claims adversarially verified (10 confirmed, 4 contested). Per-topic and integration synthesis on Opus. Full verification log in the appendix.

---

## Verdict

Both additions earn a place; neither earns the core.

**Wilderness (coast/island): GO as a terrain-gated single-user survival mode, NO-GO as a disaster app.** The positioning/DEM/breadcrumb/compass stack reuses cleanly, and open-sky GPS is the best fix the phone ever gets (~4.9 m vs 7-13 m urban). But the mesh and crowd-hazard layer, the product's actual value, is structurally dead with no population, and the urban drift-eliminator (road-graph map-matching) has no cheap substitute: its stand-in, barometric-DEM terrain matching, is weakest on exactly the flat beaches and atolls that dominate the crash scenario (165 m DRMS on the only free DEM, and canopy degrades GPS and DEM together). **Biggest risk:** shipping "coast/island mode" as a shrunk disaster app and discovering it is breadcrumb plus raw GPS in a raincoat. Scoped honestly as a solo instrument, it is nearly free.

**Drone: v1-optional-gear for the ~8% who own one, never a dependency.** Aerial nadir re-survey is the standout: top-down imagery sidesteps the perceptual aliasing that killed camera self-localization, self-georeferences, and rides local-bulk transfer, not the mesh. Scouting and burst relay work but fight the environment: video range collapses 10-20x to 0.5-1 km in the urban canyon, relay endurance is 20-46 min, and GPS denial (the scenario) attacks the drone's own hover/return-to-home safety net. **Biggest risk:** treating optional gear as load-bearing.

**Posture:** urban core primary; wilderness gated behind "no peers," drone behind ownership. Both inert-safe.

## Wilderness (coast/island) graceful degradation

**Bottom line.** Coast/island is a cheap, honest reuse for the positioning/terrain/breadcrumb layer but not for the product's core value proposition. Open-sky GPS is the best case a stock phone ever sees (~4.9 m vs 7-13 m urban) [1][2], the DEM plus coastline pack is tens of MB [3][4], and breadcrumb-return is a mature offline feature with zero R&D [5]. But the mesh and crowd-hazard layer dies with the population, and the urban drift-eliminator (road-graph map-matching) has no equal substitute: its best replacement, barometric-DEM terrain matching, is weakest exactly on the flat beaches and low atolls that dominate the crash scenario [6]. Verdict: GO on positioning/terrain/breadcrumb reuse; GO on the app as a whole only with an explicit scope cut that retires hazard-sharing and reframes "coast/island mode" as a single-user survival instrument, not a shrunk disaster app.

### Shippable now

Open-sky GPS is the answer, not the problem. GPS.gov puts smartphone open-sky accuracy at ~4.9 m (95%) [1]; urban smartphone studies show 7-13 m average with a few-hundred-meter worst case from multipath and satellite blockage [2]. Coast/island removes the two urban error sources (rebar multipath, building blockage), so the specific "crash-landed, open sky, no jamming" design center is very likely GPS-INTACT, and there is no road graph to map-match against anyway. The compass substrate improves too: roughly ±2-10 degrees clean outdoor versus ±20-90 degrees or worse near steel/rebar [7]. The engineering implication: coast/island effort should go to canopy-dropout handling, cold-start UX, and GNSS duty-cycling for battery, not to porting the urban dead-reckoning/map-matching stack.

Solo primitives that ship today with no research risk:
- **Breadcrumb / return-to-start.** Log a track while moving, then retrace it or navigate a straight bearing home, fully offline once maps are cached. Mature in Gaia GPS, onX, and Garmin Connect IQ ("Crumbs" / TracBack) [5]. This is the highest-leverage primitive: SAR data (Koester, 145k+ cases) finds roughly 60% of found-alive lost persons within 2 miles of their last known point, and the recommended strategies are "backtrack" or "stay put," not dead-reckoning toward a guessed distant landmark [8].
- **Manual celestial backstop.** Phone-as-sight-reduction-calculator apps report ~10 nm field accuracy (StarPilot, experienced navigator) [9], down to ~17 nm actual and ~30 nm considered "normal" (Celestial Navigation 360 camera-sextant) [10]. Cheap to add as a manual mode; explicitly a last-resort, not a GPS replacement.

The data pack is trivially small: SRTM 1-arc-second (30 m) is ~25 MB per 1x1-degree tile [3]; full-resolution global GSHHG coastline is ~55.9 MB (crude version ~283 KB) [4]. A single island is a fraction of one tile, and a whole archipelago is a few hundred MB.

One shippable-now cost that is not wilderness-specific but bites on first power-up: with no network A-GPS (true by design in this offline app), cold-start time-to-first-fix is 2-4 minutes typically and up to ~12.5 minutes if the almanac is stale [11].

### Frontier

**Skyline/horizon-to-DEM matching as a coarse, human-gated re-anchor. Horizon: 2-4 years, terrain-conditional.** This is the strongest CV-only re-anchor in the wild and is genuinely validated for mountainous terrain: 88% of 1,000 queries localized within 1 km over ~40,000 km2 of Switzerland using a bag-of-curves skyline descriptor [12], and 83% success at ~300 m mean error in a separate horizon-matching study [13]. The matching step is cheap enough for on-device, but the expensive step (rendering a synthetic skyline per candidate viewpoint over the search area) is done server-side in the literature [14], so a phone-only build must bake pre-rendered skyline indices into the cached tile before the trip. That fits the floating-then-anchor architecture but adds prep-time and storage. Forward on-device DEM rendering is already shipped (PeakFinder renders a whole-world skyline at interactive rates) [15]; the inverse (recover position from an observed skyline) is the unshipped, expensive direction. Critically this is a MOUNTAIN feature: fjords, volcanic islands, and cliffs benefit; flat beaches and low atolls get little to nothing, and no published benchmark exists for coastal/atoll scenes. Scope it as a terrain-conditional bolt-on to the existing coarse-reanchor gate, not the primary coast/island positioning story.

**Barometric-DEM terrain matching (TERCOM-style). ~2-3 years R&D, terrain-gated.** Real and published: the Broyles et al. 2018 AFIT study built a smartphone-only (barometer + IMU + cached DEM, no GNSS) particle-filter navigator over ~31 km of field routes, achieving 45.8 m DRMS with a 10 m DEM and 60.5 m with a 30 m USGS DEM [6]. The key differentiator is that position error does NOT grow with time/distance, unlike pure dead reckoning [6]. The frontier work is making it robust on realistically cacheable global 30 m DEMs and on tropical vegetated islands, neither of which the paper tested. Note this is a single-research-group result on non-island Ohio terrain: treat the numbers as indicative, not guaranteed, for coast/island profiles.

**Automated coastline-track matching. Speculative, 3+ years, high risk.** Matching a walked IMU/PDR track's curvature against a cached coastline vector (the road-graph-map-matching analog for shorelines) has NO published automated algorithm. The only real-world precedent is manual sea-kayak "piloting": visually following the shore and taking compass bearings on landmarks as a GPS-outage contingency [16]. This is a plausible but unvalidated R&D idea, not shippable-now.

**Automated celestial fix. Frontier.** Phone-camera star-trackers reach 0.1-0.5 degree ORIENTATION accuracy [17], but that is heading, not a lat/long position fix, and it needs 15-25 visible stars, a dark sky, and no daylight. A fully automated positional celestial fix on an unmodified phone remains a frontier item.

### Failure modes

- **Flat/low-relief terrain kills the terrain-matcher, the single load-bearing risk.** Barometric-DEM matching needs elevation signal; coastlines, beaches, and atolls (often under 3-5 m relief) are close to the worst case. The Broyles flat leg (~2 m of change over 1.4 km) is the canonical example, though the adversarial review corrects the framing: the particle cloud "spread out significantly" (uncertainty ballooned), it did not hard-diverge or lose the fix, and the route still finished ~45 m DRMS [6]. Either way, a wide uncertainty cone on flat ground defeats the purpose. General TERCOM literature independently confirms degradation to large errors on flat/repetitive terrain [18].
- **DEM choice punishes the only globally-free option.** In the same study SRTM30 was the worst performer at 165.7 m DRMS, roughly 3x worse than the 10 m DEM's 45.8 m, and 30 m SRTM/Copernicus is the only DEM realistically available for a random island [6]. Recommend Copernicus GLO-30 over raw SRTM, but its performance under this exact algorithm is untested, so expect the 60-166 m band, not the headline ~46 m, for a real island.
- **DEM vertical error in vegetation.** Copernicus GLO-30 is ~4 m RMSE globally (as good as ~2.3 m in Cape Town) but degrades to over 25 m RMSE in areas of greater-than-50% canopy cover [19]; a jungle-canopied island can carry DEM error that swamps the barometric signal. Gate the GO/NO-GO on land-cover/canopy density, not merely "is there a DEM." (A "~18.6 m California" figure in the source chain could not be independently verified; treat it as low-confidence, but the over-25 m dense-canopy figure is corroborated.)
- **Barometric weather drift.** A 2 hPa/hour synoptic pressure change induces ~16 m of spurious altitude error per hour if uncorrected [20]; the filter must co-estimate a pressure-bias state or re-reference at a known elevation. Review caveat: 2 hPa/hour is an active-weather rate, not an everyday baseline, so real drift ranges from a few to a few tens of meters per hour.
- **Barometer spec is a lab number, not a field number.** Datasheet accuracy (BMP581-class, about +/-0.3 hPa, roughly 2.5 m absolute) [21] is dominated in the field by the weather drift above, not sensor noise. Do not market "meter-level altitude" from the barometer alone; the honest deliverable is a DEM-correlation particle-filter fix at tens of meters.
- **Skyline defeated by obscuration and by flat coasts.** Fog, haze, smoke, sun glare, and waves defeat skyline detection [22], and low-relief coasts confuse beaches/docks with the true horizon; both failure modes compound exactly in the coast/island scenario, and no off-the-shelf robustness guarantee exists for smoke/haze.
- **Celestial is clock-bound and sky-bound.** 4 seconds of clock error equals ~1 nm position error [23]; an unsynced quartz clock drifts ~1 s/day, adding ~0.25 nm/day of longitude error once GPS time sync is lost [24]. It is also night/clear-sky/level-horizon-dependent throughout.
- **Canopy GPS attenuation** of ~2.8-3.5 dB RMS (up to ~9.4 dB), worse leaf-on and wet, with occasional full dropout, independent of the open-sky advantage [25].
- **Storage figure correction.** The "Copernicus GLO-30 approximately 173 GB global" figure could not be corroborated; an independent Copernicus/ECMWF data listing puts the global set at ~3.4 TB (Zarr), implying ~128 MB/tile, not "tens of MB." Use SRTM's well-documented ~25 MB/tile [3] as the planning baseline; a multi-degree archipelago could land at 1-2 GB rather than "well under 1 GB," so confirm native COG tile size before committing a spec number.
- **Adoption / "is anyone even there."** The mesh assumes peers; in a depopulated scenario there are none, so the entire hazard/relay value proposition is inert (see below).

### What dies vs what survives

**Dies with no population (structural, zero substitute found):**
- Mesh relay (BLE/LoRa): no peers to hop through.
- Crowd hazard sharing: signed/corroborated/TTL reports need population density to generate and corroborate; the research found no dataset or technique that restores this function with no one present.
- Crowd-sourced map-matching: no other travelers' traces to align to.
- Trust/TTL logic keyed to corroboration counts: nobody to corroborate.
- Road-graph map-matching: no road graph in open wilderness (structural, not merely a population effect).

**Survives / becomes primary (single-user instrument):**
- Raw GPS plus barometric altitude fused against the DEM: a legitimate PRIMARY here, not a fallback, because open sky makes the raw fix good [1].
- Breadcrumb return-to-start [5], with a SAR-informed "stay put / backtrack" default [8].
- Bearing-to-coast and bearing-to-nearest-mapped-surface-water. Water routing uses HydroSHEDS / OSM Water Layer (~90 m) [26] and must be scoped as "nearest mapped surface water body," NOT a "safe drinking water finder."
- Skyline coarse re-anchor, terrain-gated [12], reusing the same cached DEM tile the terrain-matcher already needs (genuine reuse, not a new data supply chain).
- Manual celestial backstop [9][10].
- Distress/visual signalling, plus the same anchor + uncertainty-cone UI and the same local-bulk imagery discipline (imagery never on the mesh).

**Weak link to handle honestly:** OSM trail and POI (water source) completeness is volunteer-density-driven and thinnest exactly on remote/depopulated islands (as a proxy for the variance, only 16% of urban population lives where building data is over 80% complete, while 48% is under 20%) [27]. Coast/island mode must detect sparse OSM per region and degrade to bearing/terrain-only guidance rather than render confidently wrong trail overlays. Bathymetry (GEBCO, ~450 m, 7+ GB global but a small per-region subset) is a nice-to-have for shoal awareness, not core to positioning [28].

## Drone gear tier (re-survey, scouting, relay)

**Bottom line.** The drone tier is a real, shippable-now force-multiplier for the subset of users who already own a DJI-class drone: it delivers offline aerial re-survey, route scouting, and burst comms relay with no internet. But it must stay a strictly optional gear tier, never a core dependency, because ownership is thin (~8% of US adults) and the GPS-denied/urban-disaster conditions this app targets attack the drone's single weakest point, the autonomous safety systems (return-to-home, stable hover) that all assume good GPS. Re-survey and relay are the strongest of the three roles; both inherit crash, wind, and battery risk on top of the "is anyone even there with a drone" adoption problem, and one heavily-marketed accuracy figure in this space is fabricated.

### Shippable now

**Aerial re-survey (laptop/hub-tethered, not phone-native).** A Mavic 3 Enterprise-class drone flies a grid on 36-45 min of flight time per battery (up to 24-32 km flight distance) and captures nadir stills with GPS/IMU-tagged EXIF [1]. Offline fast-mapping (Pix4Dreact) stitches a 2D orthomosaic locally with no cloud in "minutes" (vendor-asserted; see failure modes) versus hours for full 3D photogrammetry, which independent benchmarks put at 3.5-8.5 hr for ~1,000 images on an 8-core/64GB machine and community reports push to 20-60+ hr on low-overlap or full-resolution sets [2][3][4]. Pix4Dreact's minimum spec is modest (i3-class CPU, 4GB RAM), but it is still a laptop/hub: there are zero known phone-native orthomosaic pipelines as of 2026, so this role breaks the phone-mass assumption [2]. Georeferencing rides the onboard non-RTK GPS at ~1-3 m absolute error (2+ m documented against ground truth; vertical error 2-3x worse), which is good enough to align a disaster orthomosaic against the cached OSM road graph but not survey grade [5]. RTK reaches cm accuracy but requires either an internet-fed NTRIP stream (absent in the target scenario) or a physically carried local base station [6][7]. GSD is roughly 1.5-2 cm/px at 60 m with a 20MP sensor, sub-1 cm at 50 m with a premium full-frame payload, with real disaster sUAS imagery averaging ~3.7 cm/px in practice, plenty to see blocked roads, collapsed structures, and debris fields [8]. The nadir modality is the core justification: top-down views (roof shapes, debris footprints, road-network layout) are far more mutually distinguishable than the repetitive ground-level facades that killed automatic camera self-localization in this project, and the drone self-georeferences via its own GPS/IMU rather than needing scene recognition [9]. Data volumes fit the local-bulk-only rule: ~420 images per flight, hundreds of MB to a few GB raw, finished GeoTIFFs commonly 1-4 GB (tens of GB uncompressed), moved drone-to-phone at 20-30 MB/s over local WiFi (QuickTransfer), never over the mesh [10][11][12]. Validated in the field: a volunteer team mapped 3,700 acres after Hurricane Helene with a Mavic 3 Enterprise [13].

**Route scouting (real-time, range-limited).** A Mini 4 Pro or Mavic 3 gives 34-45 min flight time and covers ~2 sq km per mission [14][15]. The pilot consumes an FHD live feed on the phone or a dedicated controller; O4 link latency is 15-20 ms minimum in goggles/racing mode, with realistic phone-app glass-to-glass delay higher and unpublished [16]. The binding limit is range: the 10-20 km open-field spec collapses to roughly 0.5-1 km in obstructed urban terrain (buildings, rebar, RF congestion), a 10-20x reduction in exactly the environment this app targets [16][17]. Within one battery it can visually confirm a blocked road, collapsed span, or clear detour. Night/smoke work needs a thermal payload (Mavic 3T, ~$4,000-5,000, an enterprise SKU, not the sub-$1,000 consumer tier), and thermal does not see through thick smoke or dense fog and loses contrast when target and ambient temperatures converge [18][19].

**Comms relay (burst, not persistent).** Radio LOS scales with the square root of antenna height: D(km) ≈ 3.57(√h1 + √h2) [20]. A ground node at 1.5 m has a ~4.4 km horizon; putting one end on a drone at 100 m adds ~35.7 km, giving a ~40 km geometric ceiling versus ~9 km for two ground nodes, a ~4-5x multiplier before terrain blockage [20]. A real hobbyist Meshtastic drone-repeater demo at 380 ft (116 m) achieved ~1-3 mi urban and 3-5 mi rural links [21]. A peer-reviewed UAV-LoRa disaster simulation flew relays at 60 m and held end-to-end packet reception above 0.9 with 8-12 UAVs serving 60 ground nodes, degrading to >20% loss as the node-to-UAV ratio rose [22]. Endurance is the constraint: 20-46 min untethered, and a published prototype carrying a ~2.3 kg all-up payload flew only 20 min, so this is a raise-a-link-for-tens-of-minutes burst tool, not infrastructure [23][24]. Tethered platforms (Elistair-class) sustain ~24 hr at ~60 m but are a distinct hardware class (ground station, tether spool, mains power), not backpack gear [25]. Carrier-scale prior art is strong and FAA-cleared: AT&T's Flying COW flies at 200-550 ft covering ~40 sq mi, Verizon's HAWK covers a 5-7 mi radius from ~300 ft, and a tethered "cell tower in the sky" was approved over Puerto Rico after Hurricane Maria [26][27][28].

### Frontier

**Re-survey (1-3 years).** Phone or edge-device (Jetson-class) processing collapsing "minutes on a laptop" to minutes on the same phone running the map app, plus onboard VIO/SLAM precise enough to auto-georeference GPS-denied flights. Today, GPS-denied capture is real and shipped (DJI "Attitude Mode" via optical-flow/VIO) but yields locally-consistent, relative-only geometry with no absolute geotag, so a GPS-denied flight's orthomosaic must be manually pinned to the OSM map afterward, a meaningful field-workflow cost [29]. Neither phone-native processing nor auto-georeferenced GPS-denied capture is proven at consumer-drone-linked-to-phone scale.

**Scouting (3-7 years).** Autonomous BVLOS scouting missions and multi-hop drone relays to restore video/telemetry range in obstructed urban cores. These are blocked more by regulation and mesh-relay engineering than by sensor technology.

**Relay (3-7 years).** Tethered-persistence trickling down toward backpack weight. Today the 24-hour tethered capability is a vehicle/expedition-cache item, not a phone-mass accessory; closing the endurance gap without the tether would require battery/payload advances not yet in the consumer tier.

### Failure modes

- **GPS-denied conditions attack the drone's core safety net.** Loss of GPS simultaneously removes positioning, return-to-home, and stable hover; signal/GPS loss accounts for ~25% of flyaway incidents, and the FAA logged 312 GPS-interference incidents in 2022 causing uncontrolled drift [30]. The reassuring ~1-in-1,000,000-flight-hour flyaway rate is measured on professional operators in normal GPS conditions and does not transfer to this project's jamming/urban-canyon scenario [30].
- **Wind margin is thinner than the spec.** Rated Level 5-6 tolerance (~24-31 mph) is a best-case figure (steady wind, fresh battery, no payload, skilled pilot); real safety margin in gusty, storm-adjacent conditions is materially lower [31].
- **Recharge cadence is a bottleneck.** Each battery takes 58-101 min to recharge; genuine off-grid recharge via a portable power station plus solar panel runs 1.35-2.8 hr for a full charge, so sustained operation needs multiple batteries or a dedicated power station [32].
- **Contested and refuted figures.** The frequently-cited RTK "40-60% survey-time reduction" is unsourced and should be dropped entirely, not repeated [6]. RTK's "±2 cm / ±3 cm" conflates relative (internal) with absolute accuracy: DJI's own spec is better (1 cm + 1 ppm) for relative, but absolute accuracy without ground control points can degrade to 26-76 cm [7]. Pix4Dreact's "minutes vs. hours" and flat-terrain optimization are vendor-asserted with no independent benchmark found; treat as plausible, not measured [2].
- **Thermal has hard limits.** It is blind to thick smoke and dense fog and washes out when target and ambient temperatures are close, undercutting the night/low-visibility scouting pitch [19].
- **Adoption is the structural weakness.** DJI holds ~70-90% market share, but the ownership base is thin: ~8% of US adults, ~1.87M registered recreational drones against a 335M population, skewing 96% male and 35-54 [33][34][35]. This forces the entire tier to be optional; the core mesh must function with zero drones present.
- **No dense-urban/NLOS relay data exists.** The 60 m / AE-PRR >0.9 result and the mile-range Meshtastic demo were both in open or rural conditions; no source measures relay performance amid multipath and NLOS from collapsed structures, so the real urban-disaster figure is likely substantially worse than cited [22][24].
- **Two workflow costs specific to this app.** Video range collapses to 0.5-1 km in the urban terrain that matters most [17], and a GPS-denied orthomosaic cannot auto-place onto the OSM graph, requiring manual feature-pinning after every degraded flight [29].

## Wilderness (coast/island): GO / NO-GO

**Recommendation: GO, but only as a terrain-gated single-user survival MODE, not a shrunk disaster app.** The honest framing is (a) not (b): coast/island is a cheap graceful-degradation mode that mostly REUSES the existing positioning stack, not a costly separate build. It becomes a bad idea only if we let it masquerade as "the disaster app, smaller," because the product's actual value (the mesh + crowd-hazard layer) is structurally dead with no population, and there is no cheap substitute for the urban drift-eliminator.

The load-bearing insight: in open wilderness we LOSE the road-graph map-matcher (the thing that kills IMU drift in the city) but we GAIN the best GPS the phone ever sees. Open-sky smartphone GPS is ~4.9 m 95% (W1) versus 7-13 m urban with hundred-meter multipath spikes (W2), because rebar multipath and building blockage are gone. So the architecture inverts cleanly: raw GPS moves from "periodic re-anchor" to PRIMARY, and the floating-then-anchor machinery, uncertainty cone, and local-bulk imagery discipline all survive unchanged. This is why reuse is cheap.

**Reuses existing machinery (near-zero R&D):**
- Floating-then-anchor engine + uncertainty-cone UI, with raw GPS promoted to primary (open sky earns it, W1).
- Cached-tile offline map + local-bulk imagery discipline (imagery never on the mesh): unchanged.
- DEM tile cache, the SAME tile the skyline re-anchor needs (SRTM ~25 MB per 1-degree tile; a single island is a fraction of a tile, W3). Genuine reuse, not a new data supply chain.
- Compass/heading fusion, with better inputs outdoors (±2-10 deg clean vs ±20-90 near rebar, W7).
- Human-gated coarse re-anchor gate, reused by the skyline matcher.
- Breadcrumb / return-to-start: mature offline feature, zero R&D (W5), and SAR-correct (Koester: ~60% of found-alive within 2 miles of last known point; "backtrack or stay put" beats dead-reckoning to a guessed landmark, W8).

**Net-new (real work, mostly roadmap):**
- Barometric-DEM terrain matching (TERCOM-style particle filter), the attempted stand-in for road-graph map-matching: 45.8 m DRMS on a 10 m DEM, 60.5 m on 30 m, but 165.7 m on SRTM30, the only globally-free DEM (W6). ~2-3 yr R&D, terrain-gated, and untested on tropical islands. This is the substitute that does not fully substitute.
- Bearing-to-coast and bearing-to-nearest-mapped-surface-water: new UX primitive; needs HydroSHEDS/OSM Water Layer (~90 m, W26) cached. Must ship scoped as "nearest mapped water body," NOT a drinking-water finder.
- Manual celestial backstop: cheap manual mode, ~10 nm best case, ~17-30 nm normal (W9, W10). Low risk, last-resort only.
- Pre-rendered skyline index baked into tiles (the inverse render is server-side/prep-time in the literature): net-new prep pipeline + storage.
- Automated coastline-track matching: speculative, 3+ yr, no published algorithm (W16). Do not commit.

**Dies with no population (structural, zero substitute found):**
- Mesh relay (BLE/LoRa): no peers to hop through.
- Crowd hazard sharing + the signed/corroborated/TTL trust gate: needs density to generate AND corroborate. Nobody to corroborate.
- Crowd-sourced map-matching: no other travelers' traces.
- Road-graph map-matching: no road graph exists in open wilderness (structural, not merely depopulation).

**Adversarial caveats that gate the GO:**
- Flat/low-relief terrain kills the terrain-matcher, the single biggest risk. Beaches and atolls (<3-5 m relief) are near worst-case; the particle cloud balloons and the uncertainty cone widens even if it does not hard-diverge (W6). On the flat islands that dominate the crash scenario, you are left with raw GPS + breadcrumb only.
- Canopy is a double failure: GPS attenuates 2.8-3.5 dB (up to 9.4, W25) AND DEM vertical error exceeds 25 m RMSE under >50% canopy (W19), swamping the barometric signal. Both fallbacks degrade together on a jungle island. Gate GO/NO-GO on land-cover, not merely "is there a DEM."
- Cold-start TTFF is 2-4 min, up to ~12.5 min with a stale almanac, because we ship with no A-GPS (W11). First-power-up cost.
- Storage: do not quote "tens of MB" for Copernicus GLO-30 confidently; use SRTM's documented ~25 MB/tile (W3). A multi-degree archipelago may land at 1-2 GB.

**One-line verdict:** GO on a terrain-gated, single-user coast/island survival mode that reuses the positioning/DEM/breadcrumb/compass stack for near-zero cost; NO-GO on selling it as a disaster app (the mesh/crowd core is dead) and NO-GO on funding barometric-DEM or coastline matching as anything but roadmap R&D, because the drift-eliminator has no cheap substitute on the flat terrain that dominates the scenario.

## Drone tier

Strictly an OPTIONAL gear tier, never a core dependency, gated behind ownership. The core mesh must function with zero drones present. Ownership is thin (~8% of US adults, ~1.87M registered recreational drones against 335M population, DJI 70-90% share, D33-D35), and it compounds the wilderness "is anyone even there" problem. With that fixed, the three roles rank clearly.

**1. Aerial re-survey: v1-with-optional-gear (strongest role), but hub-tethered, not phone-native.** Hooks straight into Feature 1 / the shared top-down map. A Mavic 3 Enterprise-class drone flies a grid on 36-45 min per battery (D1), captures GPS/IMU-tagged nadir stills, and an offline pipeline (Pix4Dreact) stitches a 2D orthomosaic locally, self-georeferenced by onboard non-RTK GPS at ~1-3 m absolute (D5), good enough to pin against the cached OSM road graph. This is the modality that justifies the whole tier: top-down roof/debris/road-layout views are mutually distinguishable, sidestepping the perceptual aliasing that killed automatic camera self-localization here (D9). It obeys the discipline exactly: 1-4 GB GeoTIFFs move drone-to-phone at 20-30 MB/s over local WiFi, never over the mesh (D10-D12). Field-validated (3,700 acres post-Helene, D13). Honest costs: it needs a laptop/hub (no phone-native orthomosaic pipeline exists as of 2026, D2), Pix4Dreact's "minutes" is vendor-asserted (full 3D is an independently-benchmarked 3.5-8.5 hr for ~1,000 images, D2-D4), and a GPS-denied flight yields relative-only geometry that must be manually pinned to the map afterward (D29). So this breaks the phone-mass assumption: treat it as a "field hub" sub-tier.

**2. Route scouting: v1-with-optional-gear, foot-user UX.** Fly ahead, is the road blocked / bridge out / path clear. Real-time FHD feed on phone; within one 34-45 min battery it visually confirms a blockage or a clear detour over ~2 sq km (D14, D15). Hooks in as a foot-user action that drops a hazard pin (blocked road) which then rides the EXISTING signed/corroborated/TTL mesh. Simplest to ship (no processing pipeline). The binding weakness is the environment we target: video range collapses from the 10-20 km open-field spec to ~0.5-1 km in obstructed urban terrain, a 10-20x cut (D17), and night/smoke work needs a thermal payload (Mavic 3T, ~$4-5k enterprise SKU) that still cannot see through thick smoke or dense fog (D18, D19). So: short-radius, one-battery, daylight-biased confirm tool.

**3. Comms relay: v1-with-optional-gear as a BURST tool, extends the LoRa mesh.** Radio LoS scales as D≈3.57(√h1+√h2); a node lifted to 100 m adds ~35.7 km horizon, a ~40 km geometric ceiling vs ~9 km ground-to-ground, roughly 4-5x before terrain blockage (D20). Real Meshtastic drone-repeater demo: 1-3 mi urban, 3-5 mi rural (D21); a peer-reviewed UAV-LoRa sim held end-to-end packet reception >0.9 with 8-12 UAVs over 60 ground nodes at 60 m (D22). Hooks in by bridging two isolated mesh islands or reaching over a collapsed-structure NLOS gap for tens of minutes. Endurance is the killer: 20-46 min untethered, ~20 min with a 2.3 kg payload (D23, D24); the 24 hr tethered capability is a vehicle/expedition hardware class, not backpack gear (D25). And no dense-urban/NLOS relay data exists, so the real urban-disaster figure is likely worse than cited (D22).

**Cross-cutting adversarial caveats:** GPS-denied conditions (the scenario) attack the drone's own safety net, removing positioning, return-to-home, and stable hover simultaneously (signal loss is ~25% of flyaways; FAA logged 312 GPS-interference incidents in 2022, D30). Recharge cadence is a bottleneck off-grid (58-101 min wall, 1.35-2.8 hr via power-station+solar, D32). And drop the marketed RTK "40-60% survey-time reduction" figure entirely: it is unsourced, and RTK absolute accuracy without ground control points degrades to 26-76 cm, not the marketed cm (D6, D7).

## Fit + v1 vs roadmap

Neither addition moves the design center. Urban / populated disaster stays primary. Both additions are inert-safe: the app works fully with no wilderness terrain and no drone present, and each is gated behind a trigger (wilderness behind "no peers / no road graph detected," drone behind ownership).

**In v1 (ships alongside the urban core, cheap reuse + light net-new UI):**
- Wilderness "solo survival mode," gated behind a no-peers/offline-wilderness state so it never dilutes the urban product: breadcrumb return-to-start, raw-GPS-primary on the existing anchor/cone UI, compass, offline DEM/topo tile cache, bearing-to-coast/water (scoped honestly as nearest mapped water body), and a manual celestial backstop. This is almost entirely reuse.
- Drone aerial re-survey as an optional field-hub tier (feeds the top-down map) and route scouting as an optional foot-user action (drops hazard pins into the existing mesh). Both are shippable-now for owners; label the re-survey's laptop/hub dependency plainly.
- Drone comms relay in v1 only as a manual, clearly-experimental "raise a burst relay" for owners; do not imply persistence.

**Roadmap (fund only if coast/island or the gear tier proves demand):**
- Barometric-DEM terrain matching (2-3 yr, terrain-gated): the real substitute for map-matching, but weakest on the dominant flat-island case, so this is conditional, not committed.
- Skyline-to-DEM coarse re-anchor (2-4 yr, terrain-conditional): reuses the coarse re-anchor gate; needs a pre-rendered skyline-index pipeline. Mountain/fjord/volcanic islands only.
- Automated coastline-track matching (3+ yr, speculative) and automated celestial position fix (frontier): do not commit.
- Drone: phone-native orthomosaic processing and auto-georeferenced GPS-denied capture (1-3 yr); autonomous BVLOS scouting and multi-hop drone relay (3-7 yr, regulation-blocked); backpack-weight tethered persistence (3-7 yr).

Net: the two additions add a survival-mode skin and an optional gear tier around the same spine (floating-then-anchor, byte mesh + local-bulk imagery, duty-cycled power, signed/corroborated/TTL trust). Nothing here justifies re-centering the product away from urban.
