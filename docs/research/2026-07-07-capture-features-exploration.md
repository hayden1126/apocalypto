# Capture Features Exploration — Re-survey Mapping & Fixed-Vantage Change Detection

_Date: 2026-07-07. Companion to the main research report. Explores two NEW capture features proposed after the CV-localization pillar was cut: (1) re-survey the changed world onto the map, and (3) fixed-vantage change detection. Automatic camera self-localization stays rejected._

**Method.** 9 sub-questions researched with live web search (Sonnet), 18 load-bearing quantitative claims adversarially verified (9 confirmed, 8 contested, 1 refuted). Per-feature and integration synthesis on Opus. Full verification log in the appendix.

**Locked constraints:** captures with no fix are stored FLOATING and snapped to global coords later; imagery (MB-GB) moves by LOCAL BULK transfer only, the byte-budget mesh carries just pointers/signatures/thumbnails; capture is user-triggered / duty-cycled (camera+CV is the top battery drain).

---

## Verdict

Both features earn their place, but not equally and not now.

**F3 (fixed-vantage change detection) is the stronger, near-shippable bet.** Its validated shape (human re-occupies a named vantage, ghost-overlay align, blink/slider human review, tiny signed mesh pointer) is low-power, dodges the perceptual-aliasing trap by being self-referential at one named place, and reuses your existing trust and floating-anchor machinery wholesale. Ship its classical core in v1. Biggest risk: it is a sparse sampling system that cries wolf. Illumination and shadow false alarms dominate naive differencing, and absence of a report is not absence of change between duty-cycled revisits. Keep detection narrow, optional, and human-confirmed, never an autonomous alert.

**F1 (re-survey) is the ambitious one.** Its headline, matching the new world against itself into relocalizable geometry, is a 2-5 year frontier, not a 2026 capability. What ships in v1 is the honest, dumb-but-useful layer: floating geotagged fresh imagery, pHash dedup, bulk sync, human overlay. Biggest risk: one false submap merge, from the same aliasing that killed automatic self-localization, silently misplaces an entire chunk of survey data that later users then trust.

**Recommended posture:** ship F3's align-and-overlay core and F1's capture-dedup-sync layer in v1, on one shared pointer-vs-bulk data model and one LocalSend-style transport with a USB-C floor. Gate every automatic re-anchor and submap weld behind RANSAC plus corroboration plus human-confirm. Treat all reconstruction and detection as human-confirmed proposers, never oracles.

## Feature 1 — Re-survey / mapping layer

**Bottom line.** The feature is real, but only as a *locally-consistent, floating reconstruction plus overlap-dependent relocalization*, never as survey-grade absolute geometry. "Match the new world against itself" is technically the standard image-based visual-localization / SfM-relocalization problem, which works well in the literature and in exactly one shipped product (Niantic Lightship VPS), yet every at-scale version builds maps and localizes on a server, and none is validated on dynamic disaster damage from a handful of phone captures [1]. What ships today is object/room-scale on-device 3D plus small-map relocalization *when there is genuine viewpoint overlap*; block-scale, fully offline, on-device re-survey is a 2-5 year frontier bet, not a 2026 capability.

### How it works

Given the locked constraints (floating-then-anchor, local-bulk imagery, byte-budget mesh, duty-cycled capture), the pipeline is:

1. **Capture (user-triggered, duty-cycled).** A user shoots a short panorama / video / photo burst of a changed segment. This is never a continuously-running scan: camera + on-device CV is the top battery drain (roughly 5-20%/hr for plain capture [26]; a casual ~5-minute photogrammetry capture then costs ~20-25 min processing and ~25% battery [25]), so capture must be discrete bursts.
2. **Register into a floating submap.** On-device VIO (ARKit/ARCore) stitches frames into a *locally-consistent* submap. Because VIO drifts on the order of meters over hundreds of meters outdoors (ARKit ~0.19 m vs ARCore ~3.98 m final error over a 513 m run [2]), this submap is stored **floating** (relative "get me back here" geometry), never as absolute position.
3. **Derive tiny signatures.** Per keyframe, extract a compact global place descriptor + a near-duplicate hash + a thumbnail. These are the only things small enough to ride the mesh.
4. **Mesh carries pointers only.** The byte-budget mesh (Bridgefy-class deployments cap messages at 384 bytes [6]) carries the signature / thumbnail / pointer / hash and the hazard note. It structurally cannot carry imagery: full map-merge data runs ~302 KB/s per agent even in optimized distributed SLAM [5], orders of magnitude over the mesh budget.
5. **Bulk-move imagery on physical contact.** Full imagery and meshes move only by local bulk transfer (phone-to-phone when devices meet, or dumped at a camp/hub).
6. **Relocalize on overlap.** A later user's device relocalizes against an earlier submap *only where real viewpoint overlap exists* (ARKit ARWorldMap ships exactly this offline save/load/relocalize model, and Apple is explicit that success depends on revisiting mapped areas [3]).
7. **Snap to global coordinates late.** When a GNSS fix or a known landmark appears, the floating submap is welded to global coordinates via a Sim(3) alignment + local bundle adjustment; ORB-SLAM3's Atlas does this submap merge in 120-287 ms with 1-6 ms place-recognition queries [4].
8. **Gate every merge.** The same perceptual-aliasing failure that got automatic camera self-localization rejected applies identically to submap place-recognition: a single false match silently misplaces or misrotates an entire submap of survey data. Every automatic weld needs RANSAC geometric verification plus corroboration / human-confirm, mirroring the corroborated-hazard-report gate [7].

### Shippable now

- **On-device 3D is real but confined to object/room scale.** LiDAR iPhones produce mesh/point-cloud scans in seconds to low minutes. But watch the accuracy claim: the widely-cited RoomPlan "~8.5 cm average" figure is **contested** and untraceable to a primary study; independent LiDAR-app testing against a terrestrial laser scanner found RMSE spanning 10-56 cm depending on the app alone [8][9]. Design for double-digit-cm error that varies ~5x by app and worsens with distance/complexity, not sub-2 cm.
- **iPhone LiDAR cannot reach street scale.** Polycam's LiDAR mode is hardware-capped at ~5 m depth and has no native GCP/RTK anchoring [10]; a road width or facade-across-a-street routinely exceeds that, so LiDAR meshing is a room/object tool, not a block tool.
- **Correction on Apple's pipeline (prior claim REFUTED).** Apple's Object Capture is *not* macOS-only: an on-device `PhotogrammetrySession` shipped on iOS 17 (2023, iPhone 12 Pro and later) and was extended to room/area scale at WWDC24, so a phone can run capture-and-reconstruct fully offline at object-to-small-area scale [11]. The real caveat: Apple's own docs say quality degrades above ~6 ft / area scale without optional Mac post-processing, which is exactly the street-segment regime Feature 1 targets.
- **Photo-mode / splat reconstruction is minutes on-device.** PocketGS trains a 3D Gaussian-splat on an iPhone 15 in ~4 min end-to-end, under 3 GB peak RAM [12]. Treat this as confirmed-for-what-it-is: one unreplicated Jan-2026 preprint, reduced quality (PSNR 23.67 dB at 500 iterations vs ~28-30 dB desktop), **A16/iOS only, no Android data**. Note that some cited "on-device" splat apps (e.g. KIRI Engine) are actually cloud pipelines and are disqualified by the offline constraint, as is RealityScan Mobile, which uploads full-resolution images to Epic's servers for the real reconstruction [13].
- **Relocalization accuracy is good where overlap exists.** State-of-the-art hierarchical localization (HLoc: SuperPoint+SuperGlue+NetVLAD) reaches ~0.26 m / 0.69 deg on Cambridge-scale scenes, but the reference map is built offline from thousands of images on a workstation, not incrementally on a phone [14]. On-device building blocks that ship today: ARWorldMap (offline, peer-to-peer, room-scale [3]) and ORB-SLAM3 Atlas multi-session merge (0.03-0.065 m ATE on EuRoC, 2.6-3.2x better than VINS-Mono [4]).
- **The one disaster precedent is offline batch.** The closest real-world analog (Ratner et al. 2019, crowd-sourced SfM of a volcanic crater) used ~1,001 mixed-camera photos from 3 groups in ~1 hour to hit terrain accuracy comparable to expert SfM/LiDAR, but processed on a workstation after the fact, not on a phone in the field [15].
- **Descriptors for dedup are cheap.** A pHash near-duplicate signature is 8 bytes (64-bit) with sub-ms compare [17]; a learned global descriptor (e.g. CosPlace 512-D, 94.5% R@1 on Pitts30k in-domain [16]) is 0.5-2 KB raw, compressible toward 128-512 bytes via PCA + int8/binary quant, at a measurable recall cost (int8 post-training quantization ~-6.9% R@1 [18]; NetVLAD PCA-reduces to 128-512 D with modest loss [19]).
- **Storage is not the bottleneck; transfer time is.** Phones ship 128-512 GB [21], a panorama is 5-25 MB and 4K video is 130-360 MB/min (HEVC vs H.264) [20], while real-world Wi-Fi Direct app-layer throughput is only ~16-20 Mbps [22] after a 5-9 s discovery/handshake [24]. AirDrop is faster but its "typical" rate is **contested**: the 30-50 Mbps figure likely understates it (AWDL benchmarks and field tests put sustained transfers in the low-hundreds of Mbps), though all figures are best-case, same-OS, benign-RF consumer benchmarks [23]. Budget tens of seconds to a few minutes to move a single-vantage set (tens to low-hundreds of MB) per encounter.

### Frontier

- **On-device dense reconstruction near real time (1-3 yr).** A SIGGRAPH Asia 2025 challenge targets full 3DGS reconstruction in 30-60 s [27], and mobile-targeted work (Mobile-GS, PocketGS) is being demonstrated on phone-class silicon (iPhone 15 Pro, Pixel 8) [28]. This is the building block that could let a phone build a dense, relocalizable segment reconstruction in the field without a cloud SfM step.
- **Federated / decentralized mapping (2-5 yr).** OpenFLAME proposes a federated VPS letting independent organizations share crowdsourced maps without one cloud silo [29], the architectural shape a relief coalition would want. Swarm-SLAM is the closest academic system to this scenario (explicitly built for intermittent, ad-hoc, partially-connected networks, 20-40% lower ATE and 30-60% less comms than decentralized baselines [30]), and asynchronous pose-graph optimizers (ASAPP, iMESA) prove convergence under large communication gaps in principle [31]. The gap: all are demonstrated on robot teams with *occasional live links*, not humans meeting once every few hours with zero connectivity between, and none addresses staleness or mesh-starved map distribution.
- **Sub-100-byte descriptors (2-3 yr, needs bespoke validation).** TeTRA-VPR (1-bit binary embeddings, 2-bit ternary backbone [32]) and HDC hypervector approaches [33] promise NetVLAD-class discrimination in a mesh-budget footprint, but every figure is on driving/campus datasets, with no phone-CPU thermal/battery numbers and zero disaster-domain evaluation.
- **Cross-platform transport (uncertain).** iOS 26 shipped a public Wi-Fi Aware (NAN) API and Google shipped a Quick Share↔AirDrop bridge [34][35], the first cracks in the iOS/Android wall, but Wi-Fi Aware cross-OS discovery is documented-broken in Apple's own developer forums and the AirDrop bridge is a single-vendor reverse-engineering hack requiring the iPhone's insecure "Everyone for 10 minutes" mode. Do not architect around either yet.

### Data model

**Rides the mesh (must fit a ~237-384 byte budget [6]):**

| Item | Size | Note |
|---|---|---|
| pHash near-dup signature | 8 bytes (64-bit) [17] | exact/near-duplicate suppression, sub-ms compare |
| Compact global place descriptor | 128-512 B (PCA + int8/binary quant); 0.5-2 KB raw [16][19] | a full 512 B descriptor already exceeds one 237 B frame, so it fragments across frames or ships as a bulk-contact attachment |
| DBoW visual-word code | ~3 B/word vs 32 B raw ORB [36] | ~10x compression for loop-closure signature |
| Thumbnail + pointer/hash + hazard note | tens-hundreds of bytes | the actual mesh payload |

AnyLoc-class descriptors (49,152 D raw [37]) are categorically too large for the mesh and must be compressed or kept device-local.

**Moves by local bulk only (MB-GB):**

| Item | Size |
|---|---|
| Panorama | 5-25 MB [20] |
| 4K video | 130-360 MB/min (HEVC/H.264) [20] |
| Object/room mesh or point cloud | tens of MB; street-segment textured mesh extrapolates to hundreds of MB-low GB |
| Submap / ARWorldMap blob + map-merge data | ~302 KB/s per agent in distributed SLAM [5], orders of magnitude over the mesh |
| **Transport** | Wi-Fi Direct ~16-20 Mbps app-layer [22]; USB-C 480 Mbps-40 Gbps as a hub fallback [38] |

The split is load-bearing: the mesh is a *coordination and dedup layer* ("has this corner already been surveyed, and is there a fresher capture to fetch on contact?"), never a transport layer for pixels.

### Failure modes

- **Staleness (the binding constraint no frontier work solves).** The reference is a decaying asset: the world keeps changing week to week (fire spread, new collapse, flood extent). Long-term VPR benchmarks (RobotCar/CMU Seasons) exist *precisely because* pose accuracy drops sharply when query conditions diverge from the reference [39]. A crowd-built reference must carry TTLs and a re-survey cadence, and relocalization confidence must decay with reference age, or a later user confidently localizes against geometry that no longer exists.
- **Perceptual aliasing (the same failure that killed auto self-localization).** A homogenized ruinscape produces confident false matches. For *reconstruction merges* this is catastrophic: one false submap match misplaces/misrotates an entire survey chunk, so auto-merge needs RANSAC + corroboration + human-confirm [7]. For *descriptor dedup* the stakes are asymmetric but real: a false negative just wastes bulk-transfer bandwidth, but a false positive wrongly declares two different collapsed buildings the same place. Critically, **no source evaluates any of these descriptors on post-disaster/ruinscape imagery** — every recall figure is from urban-driving or campus benchmarks [42]. Extrapolating VPR recall to a bombed-out scene is unverified, and confidence there is low.
- **Viewpoint jitter and thin overlap.** Classical SfM fails *catastrophically, not gracefully*, below an overlap threshold (COLMAP "fails catastrophically for triplets with the least overlap") [40]. The product needs a capture-UI that coaches ~30-50% inter-frame overlap [41] and a hard fallback to floating/local-pose-only when overlap is marginal, rather than emitting a confident-but-wrong global fix.
- **Power.** Reinforced throughout: camera + CV is the dominant drain (~5-20%/hr plain, higher for continuous AR/CV) [26], and a single capture-and-process cycle can cost ~25% battery and 20-25 min [25]. Capture and reconstruction must be short, explicit, user-triggered operations; bulk transfer is a separate screen-on operation, never a background mesh function.
- **Cross-platform (iOS↔Android) reality.** ARWorldMap merge is Apple-only; ARCore Persistent Cloud Anchors need live internet and Google's cloud [44], so neither is a portable offline snapping mechanism. There is no reliable native cross-OS bulk-transfer path (AWDL/MultipeerConnectivity is Apple-proprietary [46], Wi-Fi Direct is Android-only in practice). The load-bearing default is SoftAP + plain HTTP (the LocalSend model: same-Wi-Fi/manual-hotspot, no internet [45]), with QR handoff or a USB-C cable [38] as the always-available floor, and reconstruction formats/pipelines still differ per platform. A mixed fleet works, but only if the design assumes the degraded manual path, not a seamless automatic handoff.
- **Anchoring honesty.** Even fully merged, single-session outdoor drift without loop closure has been reported near 60 m over a long run; published cm-dm multi-session numbers are a floor under ideal loop-closure conditions, not a guarantee [4]. A floating reconstruction stays floating until a trusted GNSS fix or landmark snaps it, and its uncertainty must be shown, consistent with the uncertainty-cone spine.

## Feature 3 — Fixed-vantage change detection

**Bottom line.** This feature is real, but only in a narrow, human-supervised form. The validated shape is: a person physically re-occupies a known vantage (hilltop, camp, checkpoint), the app helps them re-frame against the prior shot, and the two captures are aligned and presented for a human to compare (blink/slider overlay), optionally flagged by a *narrow, single-class* detector (water extent, smoke) where published evidence exists. A general, fully-automatic, multi-hazard "diff the ruinscape and alert" detector is **not** shippable: there is no domain-matched benchmark for ground-level oblique disaster change detection [19], every strong change-detection F1 in the literature comes from nadir satellite imagery that does not transfer [1][24], and the dominant real-world signal in naive outdoor differencing is illumination/shadow false alarms, not real hazards [3][23]. Treat F3 as a capture-align-and-overlay tool with optional narrow classifiers, not an autonomous hazard oracle.

### How it works

Pipeline under the locked constraints:

1. **Capture (user-triggered, duty-cycled).** The operator stands at a vantage and presses a button; the phone shoots a panorama or fixed set of photos. No continuous scanning: camera + on-device CV is the top battery drain, so capture is one-shot per revisit. On the *second and later* visits, the app fades the prior capture as a semi-transparent "ghost overlay" over the live preview so the human physically re-occupies the framing before the shutter fires — this is a solved, shipping, no-network, low-power mechanic on iOS today (GhostFrame, AlignShot, Camera Overlay) [10][11]. Putting the human in the alignment loop is what makes the hard registration problem tractable on a phone.

2. **Floating anchor, snap later.** Per the locked positioning rule, a vantage is stored as a *named local anchor* ("Checkpoint North overlook"), FLOATING until a GNSS fix or a known landmark snaps it to global coordinates. Crucially F3 does not need global position to work: change detection is *self-referential over time at one place*, so the vantage can be re-occupied and diffed entirely in a local frame and geo-referenced opportunistically afterward.

3. **Align.** Feature-based registration (ORB/AKAZE + RANSAC homography) warps the new capture onto the reference; for a pure pan with negligible translation, FFT phase-correlation is an even cheaper fallback [9]. This runs on-device in ~0.15–0.3 s for a single-shot homography estimate [9]. Registration is validated the way repeat-photography science does it: measure residual displacement in *known-static* regions (distant structures) and reject/re-shoot if it exceeds threshold [27].

4. **Difference + present.** Do NOT trust raw pixel differencing (see Failure modes). Instead: (a) robust/ordinal or significance-test differencing that sets an explicit, tunable false-alarm rate [3], and/or (b) a narrow single-class detector where evidence supports it (water/flood extent, smoke), and (c) always surface the aligned pair as a human-reviewable blink/slider overlay. The output is a hazard candidate a human confirms — not an automatic alert.

5. **Share (mesh = pointers only; imagery = bulk).** The byte-budget mesh (237-byte signed hazard notes) carries only a tiny pointer: vantage ID, timestamp, change class, a coarse confidence, and a thumbnail hash/signature — never the imagery. The MB–GB panorama stacks move ONLY by local bulk transfer when devices physically meet or dump at a camp/hub.

### Shippable now

- **Alignment is a solved mobile problem.** ORB/AKAZE + RANSAC homography estimation runs in ~0.16–0.33 s on phone-class CPU; some optimized Android panorama pipelines report sub-0.05 s per frame [9]. SIFT-family features are explicitly robust to illumination, scale, and rotation and tolerate *minor* viewpoint shift, degrading only under large viewpoint change; ORB is ~100× faster/cheaper but produces more mismatches — a real accuracy-vs-power dial for battery-limited use [9]. Live ghost-overlay re-occupation apps already ship on iOS and remove most of the registration burden by getting the human back on the spot before the shutter [10][11].
- **Fixed-camera repeat photography is decades-mature.** Glacier, vegetation, and rock-slope monitoring prove fixed-vantage repeat capture + automated differencing works over long spans, including an automated daily-capture rock-slope system with server-side auto-processing [4]. *Caveat (contested):* that precedent's robustness actually comes from full 3D Structure-from-Motion point-cloud differencing with camera calibration and vegetation masking, **not** the lightweight 2D homography diff this framing implies [4][7]. The mature-precedent claim is confirmed; the "it's just 2D warping" reading is not — the validated implementation is heavier than a phone diff.
- **Narrow single-class detectors that hit usable operating points:**
  - *Flood/water extent* is the strongest fit — high-contrast boundary, genuinely static camera. DL water segmentation from fixed cameras reaches MAE ~0.96–2.66 cm vs gauges and F1 > 0.9 for extent extraction [12][13]; a landmark-classification river system hit 91–97% balanced accuracy across four sites and ~0.94 correlation to gauges over a year — but it *requires* a fixed, non-rotating field of view and pre-surveyed landmark elevations, a stricter constraint than a hand-held revisit [14].
  - *Wildfire smoke* from fixed cameras is mature but tuned for continuous CCTV, not sparse revisits: reported operating points span 80.4% detection at 1.13% false-positive rate, 91.6% accuracy at 7.5% FPR, and a deployed ~130-camera network at <1 false positive per camera per day with ~15 min time-to-detection [15][16][5]. These numbers likely degrade under sparse duty-cycled snapshots and more scene variety (haze, dust resembling smoke).
- **Ground-level structural/road-blockage change is immature.** The most relevant repeat-imagery road-damage differencing result is F1 = 0.594 (best of compared methods) — coin-flip-adjacent, not field-reliable [17]. Terrestrial building-damage detectors exist (lightweight YOLOv5) but are evaluated on curated data, not offline disaster conditions [25], and satellite/UAV debris segmentation itself only reaches ~40–57% mAP/IoU [25][26].
- **Human-logistics of crowdsourced repeat capture is proven at scale.** Chronolog (phone-bracket kiosks at fixed vantages) has >100,000 submissions across 48 US states and 10 countries since 2017 — but it does *zero* automatic detection, only human-viewed timelapses [18]. It validates capture-and-align, not the detection half.

### Frontier

- **On-device learned change proposers (2–4 yr).** TinyCD-class siamese U-Nets are small — ~300K params, 1.45 GFLOPs, F1 91.05% on LEVIR-CD [2]. That compute is phone-plausible (comparable to MobileNetV2's ~0.3 GFLOPs), but "real-time on a phone NPU" is **unproven** — no on-device latency/power benchmark exists, and no benchmark on ground-level imagery exists [2]. Two hard caveats: (i) a plain U-Net matches or beats these transformer/lightweight CD nets under fair retraining (U-Net F1 84.17 vs ChangeFormer 77.75 / TinyCD 78.53 on WHU-CD), so architecture sophistication buys little [1]; (ii) TinyCD-family nets are not temporal-order invariant unless trained for it — swapping "before"/"after" can drop F1 sharply, a concrete field failure when captures get mis-registered or mislabeled [2]. Realistic role: a change-*region proposer* a human still confirms.
- **The blocking gap is data, not models.** Zero domain-matched benchmarks exist for ground-level oblique fixed-vantage disaster change detection [19]. The closest real analogs — Sakurada & Okatani's TSUNAMI street-scene set (F1 ≈0.64–0.72) and Alcantarilla et al.'s SLAM-registered street-view CD (F1 ≈0.77) — sit 15–25 F1 points below nadir aerial numbers and needed heavy 3D/SLAM registration to fight viewpoint jitter (per adversarial verification; primary URLs not captured, treat as low-confidence). This is an *understudied* problem, not merely an evidence gap.
- **3D-aware alignment to kill the re-occupation requirement (3–7 yr).** Parallax-tolerant multi-homography/mesh warping [7], deep 3D-reconstruction stitching (PIS3R) [21], and mobile Gaussian Splatting (Mobile-GS, ICLR 2026) [20] point toward synthesizing the *exact* reference viewpoint from a nearby-but-imperfect re-occupation, removing the "stand on the same spot" constraint entirely. But current mobile GS targets rendering, not fast single-new-photo-in / diff-out reconstruction, and there is no evidence of a latency/battery budget compatible with a duty-cycled disaster app [20][21]. AR relocalization can't rescue this either: ARCore/ARKit visual-inertial tracking tops out around 3–5 cm and generally assumes cloud anchors this offline product can't use [22].

### Data model

**Rides the mesh (tiny, ≤237 B hazard note + small pointer):**
- Vantage anchor ID + local/floating pose (or global coords once snapped)
- Capture timestamp + revisit index
- Change class enum (water-up / smoke / new-collapse / new-blockage / no-change)
- Coarse confidence + human-confirmed flag
- Signature (author key) + TTL for expiry
- Content hash / thumbnail hash pointing to the bulk imagery (a few KB thumbnail at most; the mesh carries the *hash/signature*, and a thumbnail only if it fits the budget)

**Moves by local bulk only (MB–GB, phone-to-phone on physical contact or camp/hub dump):**
- Full panorama stacks / photo sets per epoch (multi-MB each; a multi-visit time series is tens of MB to GB)
- Aligned reference frame(s) for a vantage
- Any on-device CD model weights distributed to peers — TinyCD-class is ~300K params (single-digit MB), so a model itself *could* ride bulk sync, not the mesh [2]

The mesh answers "did the hazard at Checkpoint North change, and should I detour?" in a handful of bytes; the imagery that proves it only travels when devices meet.

### Failure modes

- **Staleness (the world keeps changing).** Fixed-vantage diffing is only as fresh as the last revisit, and revisits are sparse/duty-cycled by design (power). A vantage diffed twice a day can miss a collapse or fire jump that happens and evolves between visits. TTL-expiring mesh notes bound how long a stale "all clear" is trusted, but the deeper limit is that F3 is a *sampling* system — absence of a report is not evidence of no change. Continuous fixed-camera systems get their low false-alarm numbers from dense video feeds [5][15]; those numbers should be assumed to worsen under sparse revisits.
- **Perceptual-aliasing avoidance.** F3 sidesteps the aliasing trap that killed automatic camera self-localization: it is *self-referential over time at one named place*, never matching imagery against a homogenized ruinscape to infer position. It must stay that way — the vantage identity comes from the human/anchor, not from image matching. Do not let F3 imagery back-door into establishing global position; that reintroduces confident false fixes.
- **Viewpoint jitter / parallax (the core physics limit).** A single global homography is only valid for a purely rotating camera or a planar scene; any translation + depth variation produces ghosting at depth boundaries [7]. Sub-pixel misregistration alone can inject >50% spurious difference signal in detailed scenes, and ~0.2 px accuracy is needed to hold change error under 10% (medium/low confidence — these are aerial-derived rules of thumb) [6]. Rule of thumb: lateral re-occupation error becomes visible once it exceeds ~1/1000 of the distance to the nearest object (low confidence) [8] — so a far-field ruinscape from a hilltop is forgiving, but *foreground* rubble or a road right below the vantage will fake "change" if the photographer stands 0.5–1 m off. Mitigations: ghost-overlay re-occupation [10][11], validate on static regions [27], difference at a coarse/robust scale, and lean on human overlay review rather than automatic pixel diffs.
- **Illumination/shadow = the dominant false-alarm source.** Naive pixel differencing under an illumination shift lights up as change nearly everywhere — a ~100% false-positive area (qualitative characterization, **not** a measured statistic — do not quote it as one) [3]. This is well established across decades of classical CD, and the standard video CD benchmark carries a dedicated shadow false-positive metric (FPR-S) precisely because shadows remain the top failure mode after 10+ years of work [23]. Raw diffing outdoors is unusable without ordinal/rank measures, significance tests with tunable false-alarm rate, or shadow/albedo correction [3]. This is why "cry wolf" is the default outcome of a naive implementation and why human confirmation is mandatory.
- **Power.** Camera + on-device CV is the top drain, forcing user-triggered, one-shot-per-revisit capture. Alignment (~0.15–0.3 s homography) is cheap [9]; the risk is any learned CD net whose on-device latency/battery is *unmeasured* [2], and the frontier 3D/GS reconstruction path, which has no demonstrated duty-cycle-compatible budget [20][21]. Keep detection narrow and optional; keep the mandatory path (align + overlay) classical.
- **Cross-platform (iOS ↔ Android).** The shipping ghost-overlay precedents are iOS-side [10][11]; an Android equivalent is buildable but not off-the-shelf-proven here. AR relocalization is capped at ~3–5 cm on both ARCore/ARKit and assumes cloud anchors this product can't use [22], so re-occupation must rely on the human + ghost overlay + landmark triangulation [27], not platform AR. Imagery bulk transfer between iOS and Android is a separate, real interop cost the mesh design must own (the mesh only moves the tiny pointer; the MB–GB imagery interop is the harder cross-platform problem).

## Shared capture infrastructure

The single most important shared decision is already locked and it is load-bearing for both features: **the mesh is a coordination and dedup layer, never a transport for pixels.** Both features split identically into a tiny signed pointer on the byte-budget mesh (~237-384 B) and MB-GB imagery that moves only by local bulk transfer on physical contact.

**Bulk-transport reality (measured, not aspirational).**

| Path | Real throughput | Cross-OS? | Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wi-Fi Direct | ~16-20 Mbps app-layer after a 5-9 s handshake | Android-only in practice | Android peer-to-peer |
| AirDrop / AWDL | Fast but Apple-only: iOS 27 tests moved 3.5 GB in 2m11s = **213.7 Mbps** (to iOS 26) and **683 Mbps** (iOS 27↔27); ~94 MB/s in lab | Apple-only | opportunistic same-OS fast path |
| LocalSend model (mDNS + REST/HTTPS-TLS over shared LAN or manual SoftAP) | Link-limited (no artificial cap) | **Yes (iOS/Android/desktop)** | **the load-bearing default** |
| USB-C cable | 480 Mbps (USB 2.0) to 40 Gbps (USB4) | Yes | always-available floor, RF-immune |

The briefs' "AirDrop ~30-50 Mbps" figure is **refuted upward**: budget low-hundreds of Mbps same-OS, best case. But do not architect on it, because it is Apple-only. The portable spine is [LocalSend-style SoftAP + mDNS + HTTPS](https://github.com/localsend/localsend), which depends on neither vendor's proprietary stack, with a USB-C cable as the floor that always works.

**Cross-platform (iOS↔Android) is still a wall with two fresh cracks, neither reliable yet.** [iOS 26 shipped a public Wi-Fi Aware (NAN) framework](https://9to5mac.com/2025/06/20/ios-26-to-let-third-party-apps-build-their-own-airdrop-alternative/) and Android has had NAN since 8.0, so same-app cross-OS discovery is theoretically possible, but it is [documented-broken in Apple's own developer forums](https://developer.apple.com/forums/thread/790195) today. The [Quick Share↔AirDrop bridge is now an official Google feature](https://blog.google/products/android/quick-share-airdrop/) (Pixel 10, Nov 2025, NetSPI-audited, [expanding to Pixel 9 / Samsung / Nothing through 2026](https://appleinsider.com/articles/26/02/05/airdrop-compatible-quick-share-feature-to-roll-out-to-more-android-devices-in-2026)) — a real upgrade from the briefs' "reverse-engineering hack," but it is still single-vendor-controlled, Pixel-first, and **not an SDK you can call.** Treat both as opportunistic user-side conveniences, not APIs to build on.

**Hub aggregation is how imagery actually crosses a zone with no backhaul.** A camp/hub is a device running the same LocalSend-style HTTP drop-box; couriers physically carry storage between hubs (sneakernet). The hub is also the natural home for the workstation-class batch reconstruction the phone cannot do in-field (the Ratner-2019 disaster-SfM precedent was exactly this: offline batch on a workstation).

**Storage/compression budget.** Phones ship 128-512 GB, so storage is not the bottleneck; transfer time and battery are. A panorama is 5-25 MB, 4K video 130-360 MB/min (HEVC ~half of H.264, so prefer HEVC/AV1 capture to halve transfer time). A single-vantage set (tens to low-hundreds of MB) is tens of seconds to a few minutes per encounter. Because a crowd accumulates GB fast and the reference is a decaying asset anyway, cached imagery needs an explicit **TTL + LRU eviction policy**, not unbounded retention.

**The shared pointer-vs-bulk data model (both features reuse one envelope):**

| Rides the mesh (bytes) | Moves by bulk only (MB-GB) |
|---|---|
| pHash near-dup signature (8 B) | Panoramas (5-25 MB), 4K video (130-360 MB/min) |
| Compact global descriptor (128-512 B; a 512 B descriptor exceeds one 237 B frame, so ship its **hash** on the mesh and the descriptor on bulk contact) | Object/room meshes, street-segment textured mesh (100s MB-low GB) |
| Segment/vantage ID, timestamp, class enum, coarse confidence | Submaps / ARWorldMap blobs |
| Author signature + TTL + thumbnail hash | CD model weights (TinyCD-class ~300K params, single-digit MB → rides bulk sync, never the mesh) |

## Fit into the existing architecture

**Positioning stack: both features are consumers first, cautious feeders second.** Every capture inherits the current pose estimate *and its uncertainty* from the existing spine (GNSS-when-trusted → IMU PDR → map-match → coarse re-anchor, shown as an uncertainty cone). That inherited uncertainty is exactly what makes floating-then-snap honest. Floating-then-snap is not new machinery: it is the existing anchoring rule applied to imagery, welded to global coords later via Sim(3) + local bundle adjustment when a GNSS fix or known landmark appears.

Both features *can* feed re-anchor candidates back into positioning: F1 via submap relocalization on genuine viewpoint overlap, F3 via landmark triangulation at a named vantage. **This is precisely where the rejected automatic camera self-localization tries to sneak back in.** Perceptual aliasing in a homogenized ruinscape produces confident false fixes, and no source evaluates any of these descriptors on post-disaster imagery. So a capture-derived re-anchor must be gated identically to a hazard report: RANSAC geometric verification + corroboration + human-confirm, and it enters as a *low-priority candidate, never an authority* over the position estimate. The uncertainty cone stays the truth-telling UI. F3 must be firewalled harder: it is self-referential over time at one named place and must never back-door into establishing global position.

**Mesh + trust layer: a new payload type, zero new primitives.** Capture pointers become a message type inside the existing 237-byte signed envelope alongside hazard reports, reusing the same signing (author key), corroboration gate, and TTL expiry. The mesh answers "has this corner been surveyed / did Checkpoint North change, should I detour?" in bytes; the imagery that proves it moves only on contact. **TTL now does double duty:** it already bounds a stale "all-clear," and it must now bound reference-map staleness (F1) and stale vantage diffs (F3). Relocalization and diff confidence must *decay with reference age* — a crowd-built reference is a decaying asset, and long-term VPR benchmarks exist precisely because accuracy collapses as query conditions diverge from the reference.

**Power manager: three tiers, budgeted separately.** Camera + on-device CV is the top drain (5-20%/hr plain capture; a ~5-min photogrammetry capture costs ~20-25 min processing and ~25% battery). Register capture as explicit, user-triggered, duty-cycled *foreground* operations, never background/continuous. Tier 1 capture (camera on, seconds-minutes) is unavoidable. Tier 2 on-device CV (reconstruction/detection) is the expensive tier: keep it optional and foreground-modal with the battery cost shown, and prefer deferring it to a hub. Tier 3 bulk transfer (screen-on, radio-heavy) is a separate deliberate operation. Only the cheap mesh coordination layer stays always-on. F3's mandatory path (ghost-overlay align + ~0.15-0.3 s homography + human overlay) is cheap and stays classical; F1's reconstruction is Tier 2 and should not be in-field-mandatory.

## v1 vs roadmap

**F3 (fixed-vantage) v1 — SHIP the classical core.** It is genuinely buildable now: a named floating vantage anchor + ghost-overlay re-occupation (a shipping iOS mechanic, buildable on Android) + on-device ORB/AKAZE+RANSAC homography align (~0.15-0.3 s) + blink/slider human review + a tiny mesh pointer (vantage ID, timestamp, revisit index, change-class enum, coarse confidence, human-confirmed flag, signature, TTL, thumbnail hash). **No automatic multi-hazard detection in v1** — the mandatory path stays classical (align + human overlay). Optionally add *one* narrow classifier (flood/water extent, the strongest-evidence single class) purely as a change-region proposer a human confirms, only if it fits the power budget.

**F1 (re-survey) v1 — DEFER the reconstruction machinery, ship the dumb-but-useful layer.** Minimal useful slice: user-triggered floating photo/panorama/video capture tagged with current pose + uncertainty; pHash dedup (8 B) + thumbnail on the mesh so peers learn "this corner has fresh imagery, fetch on contact"; bulk-sync raw imagery on contact / at hubs; render as a human-viewed "what's here now" overlay pinned to the floating-or-snapped map location. **Defer on-device SfM, Gaussian-splat reconstruction, and automatic submap relocalization to phase 2+.** On iOS only, ARWorldMap "get me back here" local relocalization is an optional nicety, not a cross-platform commitment. Be honest about the framing: F1 v1 is "crowdsourced geotagged fresh imagery with dedup and human overlay," **not** "match the new world against itself" — that ambition is frontier.

**Shared infra v1.** Implement the one shared pointer envelope + bulk-blob store now (both features reuse it). Transport: LocalSend-style SoftAP + mDNS + HTTPS as the portable default, USB-C cable as the always-available cross-OS floor, native AirDrop/Quick Share as opportunistic same-OS fast paths you never depend on. A hub is just a device running the same HTTP drop-box.

**Phase 2+ roadmap (horizons from the briefs).**
- On-device dense reconstruction near real-time (**1-3 yr**) → in-field relocalizable F1 submaps (3DGS 30-60 s challenge target; PocketGS ~4 min on iPhone 15, A16/iOS-only, no Android data).
- On-device change proposers, TinyCD-class (**2-4 yr**; on-device latency/power unproven, no ground-level benchmark, not temporal-order-invariant) → F3 region proposals, still human-confirmed.
- Federated / decentralized mapping (Swarm-SLAM, OpenFLAME; **2-5 yr**) → multi-org crowd-map merge; none yet handles humans meeting once every few hours with zero connectivity between.
- Sub-100-byte descriptors (**2-3 yr**, needs disaster-domain validation) → richer VPR on the mesh.
- 3D-aware alignment (**3-7 yr**) → removes F3's "stand on the exact spot" constraint.
- Cross-OS Wi-Fi Aware once discovery actually works (**uncertain**) → automatic bulk handoff; do not plan around it.

**On the original claim:** The 5-9s figures trace to a specific, real, independently-reproduced experimental lineage — Camps-Mur, Garcia-Saavedra & Serrano, "Device-to-device communications with Wi-Fi Direct: overview and experimentation," IEEE Wireless Comm. 20(3), 2013, and the companion Conti/Delmastro/Minutiello/Paris, "Experimenting opportunistic networks with WiFi Direct," IFIP Wireless Days 2013 — and are echoed in a 2023 arXiv paper (WFD-GM, arxiv.org/abs/2307.03126) which independently cites the same 80th-percentile CDFs (Autonomous <5s, Persistent ~8s, Standard ~9s) and in a separate MDPI 2020 V2P study which reports device discovery can exceed 10s worst-case and calls this delay "an obstacle" to WFD replacing 802.11p in vehicular use — consistent with the vehicular-networking framing in the original claim. **Caveats a product team must know, going well beyond the narrow claim:**

1. **This is Android-only and stale hardware.** The base experiments used Nexus S / Nexus 5 / Nexus 6 devices circa Android 6-7. iOS has NO Wi-Fi Direct API at all — Apple uses AWDL (Apple Wireless Direct Link) under MultipeerConnectivity/AirDrop, a completely different, non-interoperable protocol. **Android and iOS phones cannot Wi-Fi-Direct-connect to each other, period.** For a disaster app that must work across a mixed fleet of donated/borrowed/whatever-phones-are-available handsets, this is a first-order design constraint, not a footnote.

2. **Real WFD throughput is modest and asymmetric.** Independent measurement (Nexus S pair, 802.11n, arxiv.org/abs/1211.2063) found ~22.6 Mbps average GO-to-client throughput over TCP; client-to-client (non-GO) traffic hairpins through the Group Owner and drops toward ~6 Mbps in multi-group topologies (Casetti et al., WoWMoM 2015, arxiv.org/abs/1412.0880). At 22 Mbps, a 1 GB imagery dump takes ~6 minutes best case; worse in the common non-GO-relay case.

3. **AWDL/AirDrop is faster but wildly variable and improving under Apple's control, not yours.** The AWDL protocol paper (Bare et al., MobiCom '18, arxiv.org/abs/1808.03156) measured up to ~650 Mbps PHY-layer throughput between AWDL-capable Macs — but that's laptop-class radios/antennas. Real iPhone-to-iPhone AirDrop field tests cluster around 100-300 Mbps (12.5-33 MB/s) for most of the last decade, with a step-change to ~680 Mbps/85 MB/s reported only in iOS 26/27 (2026) betas and ONLY when both devices run the same new OS version — i.e., throughput depends on both devices being current-gen and same-OS, which will not hold in a disaster scenario with heterogeneous, possibly years-old phones.

4. **Wi-Fi Aware (NAN)** discovery is genuinely faster (millisecond-scale per vendor/AOSP docs, source.android.com/docs/core/connect/wifi-aware) but I could NOT find independent, measured (non-vendor-marketing) throughput or real bulk-transfer numbers for it — flag this as low-confidence/thin evidence. It's also Android 8.0+ only and not universally implemented by OEMs, and again has zero iOS interoperability.

5. **User-authorization friction is real and unremovable without rooting.** WFD requires an explicit accept/PIN prompt per connection on stock Android; every academic "autonomous" WFD scheme in this space (WD2, WFD-GM, Shahin & Younis) works around this via app-level pre-shared Service Discovery credential exchange or by requiring rooted devices — meaning a shippable-now consumer app inherits a manual-tap-per-pairing UX, which matters for a "hub/basecamp aggregation" flow where many phones dump to one collector in sequence.

6. **The only broadly cross-platform local bulk-transfer path is plain Wi-Fi hotspot/AP mode** (one phone as AP, any other phone as a normal Wi-Fi client) — clunkier UX, and known to be a heavy battery drain (Sharma et al. "Cool-Tether" NSDI-adjacent 2009; Jung et al., INFOCOM 2014) since 802.11 AP mode has no power-save provision for the host. This is the fallback most likely to actually work Android<->iOS at a camp/hub, at the cost of battery and a manual "join this network" step for the recipient.

**Net for design:** plan the byte-budget mesh exactly as scoped (signatures/thumbnails only), and treat "local bulk transfer" as inherently a same-platform-pair-preferred, manual, battery-costly operation — do not assume a seamless <10s automatic handoff across an arbitrary Android/iOS pair; that only reliably exists within same-OS pairs on recent hardware/OS versions.
