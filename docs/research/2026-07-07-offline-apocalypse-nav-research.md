# Offline Apocalypse Navigation — Technology Research Report

_Date: 2026-07-07. Phase: pre-design research. This report is the evidence base for a later design/spec cycle; it does not commit to an architecture._

**Method.** 20 focused sub-questions across 4 tracks were researched in parallel with live web search (Sonnet), then 24 load-bearing quantitative claims were handed to independent adversarial verifiers (Sonnet) that tried to refute each figure. Per-track synthesis and the cross-cutting integration were done on Opus. Verifier outcome: 14 confirmed, 10 contested, 0 refuted. "Contested" means the headline number is real but conditional (measurement setup, posture, environment) — those caveats are carried into the text. Every non-obvious claim carries a source; the full verification log is in the appendix._

**Scope decisions driving this report:** primary threat models are natural mega-disaster and civil unrest/war; pandemic/collapse is the easy (infra-intact) case; nuclear/EMP is a stretch investigation. Platform is phone-first with cheap optional companion hardware (LoRa, solar, radio). Each track reports what is shippable now and where the frontier is.

---

## Executive summary

**Verdict: buildable, but only if you invert the pitch.** The defensible product is an offline map with a self-contained dead-reckoning position and a phone-to-phone hazard layer. The seductive premise, "point your camera and recognize the ruined street from pre-disaster photos," is not supported by evidence and should be cut from v1.

Three capabilities are effectively solved on commodity phones today: offline vector maps plus routing at region scale (low-single-digit GB for a home region and neighbors), multi-day power autonomy on a power bank plus a small folding panel (the screen and camera/CV are the real drains, not GNSS or BLE), and short-range BLE / longer-range LoRa phone-to-phone messaging. Positioning is a drift-limited problem: IMU pedestrian dead reckoning (step counting is ~99.5% accurate) continuously snapped to a pre-cached OpenStreetMap graph is usable, provided periodic absolute resets arrive. The load-bearing unknown is that no validated multi-kilometer, phone-only accuracy benchmark exists anywhere, and the compass fails worst (30-90 degrees of heading error) in exactly the rubble and steel this app is meant for.

**The single biggest risk is adversarial, and it is orthogonal to sensor quality.** In the civil-unrest/war model, GNSS is routinely jammed and spoofed, and the software checks that catch spoofing need connectivity we will not have, so a false fix looks valid. In the mesh, no serverless scheme is Sybil-proof (every audited app, notably Bridgefy, was fully broken), and data poisoning has no central arbiter. Better sensors fix neither. The only answers are structural: never trust a lone GNSS fix, make dead reckoning the trusted backbone, and gate every hazard report behind signing, N>=3 corroboration, and hard TTL expiry.

**Recommended shape:** a phone-first app running opportunistic anomaly-checked GNSS, then continuous low-power IMU PDR, then HMM map-matching, with an honest uncertainty cone shown to the user; plus a tiny spatial-CRDT hazard layer over BLE, with an optional LoRa-plus-solar companion node as the real range and resilience story. Duty-cycle everything. Drop CV auto-recognition, cloud VPS, UWB, Wi-Fi RTT, and any claim of Sybil-proof trust. Sell resilience, not magic.

## Scenario grounding

The four tracks quietly assume different worlds. Spelling out what actually survives in each threat model shows which findings hold and which collapse.

**Natural mega-disaster (quake / wildfire / flood).** Infrastructure loss is local and physical: cell towers, backhaul, and grid power are down or congested in the affected zone, but the GNSS space and control segments are untouched. This is the friendly adversarial case: GNSS signals are honest, so an opportunistic fix, once obtained, can be trusted. The cost is time, not truth (autonomous cold start of 2-4 minutes with no A-GPS assist, per Track A/D). What holds: GNSS-as-periodic-reset for map-matched PDR, and the whole Track A positioning ladder. What breaks: the magnetometer, precisely where it matters. Track A's rubble/steel/vehicle failure (30-90 degrees of heading error, versus 5-15 under good conditions) is acute in a collapsed built environment, and wildfire smoke plus night disable every visual and landmark correction Track B relies on. Track B's headline capability, recognizing a street whose buildings have moved, is at its weakest here and should be assumed non-functional. Power is self-supplied from hour one (Track D).

**Civil unrest / war (network shutdown, jamming, adversarial data).** This is the design driver, and it inverts the trust assumptions of every other scenario. GNSS is actively jammed and spoofed as routine practice (Track A/D document multi-country live footprints, ~2,000 spoofed aircraft in a 72-hour window, ~500% year-over-year growth). A spoofed fix looks valid, and Track D is explicit that the software-only Android spoof detection fails here because its strongest check, GNSS-versus-network-location divergence, needs the connectivity we do not have. The mesh contains a live adversary: Sybil injection, data poisoning, deanonymization, and BLE tracking (the Bridgefy break) are all in scope. What holds: self-contained IMU dead reckoning is immune to jamming and spoofing, which is why Track A's PDR-plus-map-matching backbone, not GNSS, must be the trusted primary in this profile. What breaks: any trust in a lone GNSS fix, any trust in an unsigned/uncorroborated hazard report, and any assumption of anonymity by default (deanonymization here is physical danger, not a privacy nicety). LoRa itself is jammable, so range hardware is not a trust fix.

**Pandemic / collapse (infrastructure intact).** The easy case, correctly identified. Internet, cell, GPS, and power are largely up, so the app degrades to a conventional maps application with offline and mesh features held in reserve. Every track's assumptions hold trivially; even cloud VPS works. The real product value here is pre-loading and pre-caching before conditions deteriorate, and building the user habit before it is needed. This scenario should never drive an architecture decision.

**Nuclear / EMP (stretch).** Track D's physics is sound: E1 HEMP damages long-conductor infrastructure (grid, towers, backhaul), and a phone that is off, unplugged, and uncabled is the least-vulnerable class, though survival is inferred, never measured. But Track D's conclusion ("deprioritize, a surviving phone has nothing to talk to") contains a real tension worth flagging adversarially: this app's entire premise is phone-to-phone communication that needs no infrastructure, so in a total-infrastructure-loss world the mesh layer is not useless, it is the only thing left. The correct nuance is that EMP stays a low engineering priority (Faraday-bag insurance for a spare phone and power bank, per Track D, and no "EMP-proof" marketing), but the infrastructure-free mesh and self-powered autonomy we are already building are exactly what would matter. The genuine EMP-specific problem is node-graph thinning: if a large fraction of phones in an area are bricked, mesh density (Track C's critical mean-degree constraint) may fall below viability, which is what Faraday-stored spares partially address.

## Integrated architecture

The pieces compose into one estimator, one data layer, one trust gate, and one scheduler. The unifying idea from all four tracks: **treat position as a distribution with an honest uncertainty cone, feed it cheap continuous self-contained motion, and spend scarce, untrusted absolute fixes only to shrink the cone.**

**Positioning stack (layered fusion).** The state is (position constrained to the cached OSM path graph, heading, growing covariance).

- *Layer 0, absolute and opportunistic (GNSS when available and trusted).* Use Android raw GNSS measurements offline. Run Track D's four-signal anomaly detector (AGC/C-N0 drop >5 dB, mock-provider flag, GNSS-versus-system-clock mismatch, and, only when connectivity exists, GNSS-versus-network divergence). Duty-cycle hard: one fix per 30-60 s or on demand, not 1 Hz continuous, for a roughly 10x power saving (Track D). A fix that passes the gate collapses the covariance; a fix that fails is discarded, and in the civil-unrest profile GNSS is downgraded to advisory-only.
- *Layer 1, continuous backbone (IMU PDR).* Step detection (~99.5%, Track A) plus EKF heading fusion (accel + gyro + gated magnetometer, shipping today as Android ROTATION_VECTOR and iOS CoreMotion). This runs always at low power and is the mode that survives jamming, smoke, and darkness. It also grows the covariance, so it cannot stand alone past a few hundred meters.
- *Layer 2, drift eliminator (map-matching to cached OSM).* An on-device HMM/Viterbi matcher (FMM/Meili-class, ported for a single real-time user) snaps the drifting trace to plausible graph edges, the mature commodity-grade half of the stack (Track A reports 40-97% error reduction, ~5 m corrected). This is mandatory, not optional, because it bounds lateral drift that the compass cannot. It needs an enhancement Track A flags: an explicit off-graph / open-field hidden state so that fire evacuation cutting across open ground does not break the road-network assumption.
- *Layer 3, coarse re-anchor (CV and peers, degraded and optional).* Not automatic street recognition. The trustworthy uses are Track B's disaster-robust ones: skyline-to-DEM where a horizon is exposed (no pre-disaster capture required, terrain does not collapse), short VIO segments to bridge a gap, human-in-the-loop landmark confirmation, and peer-shared trusted fixes as additional absolute anchors. Every visual mode is duty-cycled or user-triggered because Track B/D agree camera-plus-CV is a multi-watt battery killer and fails abruptly (a cliff, not a slope) in smoke and at night.

*Fallback ladder, top to bottom:* trusted GNSS reset, then PDR plus map-matching, then CV/landmark/peer re-anchor, then pure PDR with a visibly widening uncertainty cone. The user is never shown a false-confidence dot; the cone is the honest UI primitive, and it is what makes an untrusted or spoofed environment safe to operate in.

**Mesh data layer.** Transport is BLE as the only cross-platform baseline (GATT/L2CAP for annotation sync, extended advertising for unacknowledged "hazard here" beacons), foreground and screen-on, with TTL-hop relay (7 hops, BitChat-style). An optional LoRa/Meshtastic companion paired over BLE buys an order of magnitude of range and decouples the network from crowd density, at the cost of ~1 kbps and a 237-byte usable packet ceiling. The data model is the constraint that ties the layers together: a geohash-partitioned spatial delta-CRDT of hazard annotations, every edit kept under 237 bytes so it fits one LoRa packet, syncing only diffs against a pre-cached basemap (map tiles are never transmitted, only referenced). Regulatory duty cycle (roughly 100 small packets per hour per node under the US 1% limit) forces gossip to be tiny, batched, and prioritized.

**Trust layer (the gate every hazard passes through).** Nothing is believed on arrival. A report is signed (XEdDSA, the Meshtastic 2.8 pattern) to bind it to a persistent-but-anonymous key, corroborated by N>=3 independent sources (the Ushahidi rule), and expired by hard TTL (Waze-style ~30 minute auto-expiry, two "not there" votes delete) rather than by CRDT causal-stability garbage collection, because tombstones grow unbounded in a partitioned mesh that may never reach causal stability (Track C). Sybil cost is raised, never eliminated, via Rangzen-style social-graph friction (private set intersection over mutual-friend lists, no PKI). The positioning and trust layers cross-check each other: a peer reporting a physically impossible position, or a device whose own GNSS just failed the spoof gate, is downweighted as a source.

**Power manager (the cross-cutting scheduler).** Track D's decisive finding reorders priorities: the screen and camera/CV dominate draw, GNSS and BLE are minor. So the manager defaults to a dark-mode vector map with capped zoom, duty-cycles GNSS (fix every 30-60 s), scans BLE in SCAN_MODE_LOW_POWER and deliberately stops and restarts scans under 30 minutes (Android silently downgrades to opportunistic after ~30 min, and a foreground service does not exempt you), and runs CV only on user trigger or a multi-second cadence routed through the NPU. IMU PDR is the cheap always-on layer. Target autonomy: 3-5 days on a 20,000 mAh bank, effectively indefinite with a 10-20 W folding panel, which also sustains a companion mesh node that idles at 8-12 mA.

## MVP recommendation

Anchor v1 to the pedestrian in a natural disaster or civil-unrest zone. Buildable in months by leaning on solved components and refusing the unsolved ones.

**Ship in v1:**
1. Offline vector maps plus routing from pre-cut regional packages (Organic Maps / OSMAnd-class, low-single-digit GB for home region and neighbors). The pre-download UX is mature: copy it, do not reinvent it.
2. The positioning stack Layers 0-2: opportunistic GNSS with the Track D anomaly gate, continuous IMU PDR, HMM/Viterbi map-matching to the cached OSM graph, with an honest uncertainty cone in the UI.
3. A BLE hazard layer: extended-advertising beacons plus GATT annotation sync, TTL relay, spatial-CRDT edits under 237 bytes, foreground/screen-on.
4. The trust gate: signed reports plus N>=3 corroboration plus TTL expiry. Ship it and market it honestly as corroboration, not as attack-proof.
5. The duty-cycling power manager with a dark vector-map default.
6. First-class optional companion hardware: pair a LoRa/Meshtastic node and a solar panel over BLE. This is the real resilience story and it is cheap ($20-50 plus a small panel).

**Explicit DROP list for v1 (say why, plainly):**
- **Automatic CV recognition of streets/buildings against pre-disaster imagery. Drop entirely.** This is the headline that the evidence does not support: it collapses at night (fine-precision localization falls from ~85% to ~44% on appearance change alone, ~0% for pure retrieval), in smoke (unmeasured, mechanistically a cliff), and above all when buildings have actually moved, for which there is essentially zero published accuracy anywhere. DARPA SubT, the closest testbed to this threat model, was won on LiDAR multi-modal fusion; camera-only localization was not trusted even with an unlimited budget. Do not let it into the automatic positioning loop.
- **All cloud VPS (ARCore Geospatial, Niantic Lightship). Drop.** Architecturally useless in the exact outage this project targets.
- **UWB phone-to-phone. Drop, revisit in roadmap.** iOS and Android UWB stacks do not interoperate, and production UWB assumes fixed anchors.
- **Wi-Fi RTT (802.11mc/az). Drop.** Needs powered FTM-capable access points, which a grid-down zone does not have.
- **Sub-meter RTK/PPP/DGNSS. Drop.** Depends on correction streams that need connectivity or a base station.
- **Magnetic / radio fingerprinting. Drop.** Needs a pre-disruption survey of the exact area, which disaster damage may itself invalidate.
- **Foot-mounted ZUPT pod. Keep out of the phone-only core**, note it as the strongest near-term accessory bet (Track A).
- **Absolute barometric altitude and cross-device floor schemes. Drop.** Keep only relative, same-session altitude.
- **Any claim of Sybil-proof or spoof-proof operation. Drop the claim, keep the mitigations.**

*Optional stretch-into-MVP, only if cheap:* a manual "where am I" skyline-to-DEM tool where a horizon is visible (disaster-robust, no pre-capture) and human-in-the-loop landmark photo confirmation. Both are honest coarse tools; neither feeds the automatic estimator.

## Boundary-pushing roadmap

**Phase 1 (0-6 months): the MVP above.** Plus the one piece of original field research the whole project rests on (see kill criteria): generate the missing multi-kilometer, phone-only, map-matched PDR benchmark in real disaster-like terrain (rubble, steel, magnetometer disturbance). Nobody has published it; we cannot ship confidently without it.

**Phase 2 (6-18 months):**
- Crowdsourced multi-phone jamming detection and localization (Zephr-style, ~12-24 months, Track D). The highest-value near-term item because it needs no new hardware, only software plus the mesh we are already building, and it directly attacks the biggest risk.
- A purpose-built geohash-partitioned spatial delta-CRDT with signed deltas (~12-24 months, Track C); no off-the-shelf library exists.
- Distilled, quantized robust VPR (AnyLoc-lineage compressed toward MobileCLIP-scale backbones, ~1-2 years, Track B) for coarse manual re-anchor only.
- Skyline and semantic CV coarse-localization hardening.

**Phase 3 (2-4 years):**
- Secure group mesh with continuous group-key agreement (Amigo-class, peer-reviewed but not productized; the Bridgefy-to-Signal precedent puts a hardened deployable version at the far end of this window).
- UWB phone-to-phone group relative positioning, contingent on cross-platform interop landing (Track A).
- Cryptographic GNSS authentication (Galileo OSNMA, GPS Chimera) as commodity chipsets adopt it; the real structural fix for spoofing, horizon uncertain and confidence low.
- Single-image ML coarse geolocation (PIGEON / GeoLocSFT lineage), which stays km-scale ("which city or valley"), never street-scale.

**Phase 4 (5-10+ years, low confidence, a forecast not a fact):**
- Robust camera localization in combined smoke, rubble, and darkness. Track B finds no visible inflection point in the 2020-2025 trajectory. Do not stake a roadmap commitment on it.
- Alt-PNT via chip-scale atomic clocks as a companion or future-phone feature (Track D).

## Open risks & kill criteria

**Positioning (the PDR backbone).** The load-bearing unknown is that no validated multi-kilometer phone-only benchmark exists, and the physics is unforgiving (1 degree of heading error is ~17.5 m of lateral drift per km, and the compass degrades to 30-90 degrees near rubble and steel). *Kill/pivot criterion:* if Phase-1 field trials show map-matched PDR error exceeding roughly one street width (about 20-30 m) over realistic 1-3 km disaster walks even with a mandated landmark/peer reset cadence, then phone-only positioning is not street-usable and we pivot to requiring the foot-mounted ZUPT accessory. This is the most likely pillar to force a hardware dependency.

**CV in degraded environments (already de-scoped, and rightly the weakest pillar).** *Criterion to ever promote it back into the automatic loop:* a distilled robust VPR must exceed roughly 50% recall under combined night, smoke, and scene-change on a representative benchmark, and there must be at least one published measurement of relocalization against a post-collapse reference. Until both exist, CV stays a manual coarse tool. The realistic expectation is that the structural-collapse case never becomes automatic; plan as if it will not.

**Mesh transport.** *Kill BLE-only-as-primary* if density and background-execution field tests show delivery collapsing (FireChat's fame was mostly downloads, with a single credible report of actual offline mesh use, and iOS/Android both throttle background relay hard). The pivot is LoRa-companion-primary, which is why companion hardware is first-class in the MVP, not an add-on. Watch OS background-execution policy: further tightening could make foreground-only relay unworkable.

**Trust and data poisoning (unsolved, serverless).** No scheme here is Sybil-proof without a central authority (Ghost Riders ran 1,000 Waze Sybils from one Linux box). *Kill criterion:* if corroboration plus decay cannot keep the survival time of a fabricated hazard low under a modeled Sybil attack, the hazard layer becomes an attack amplifier that reroutes people into danger, and it must degrade to read-only-from-socially-trusted-peers with heavy rate-limiting. If we cannot bound poisoning, we constrain the social graph hard rather than ship an open crowd layer.

**GNSS trust (the cross-cutting biggest risk, a standing design constraint, not a future gate).** Offline spoof detection cannot catch matched-power multi-band spoofing, because its strongest check needs connectivity, and multi-constellation resilience is defeated by a sufficiently powerful all-civil-band spoofer. *Constraint:* GNSS is advisory-only in the civil-unrest profile from day one, with PDR-plus-map-matching as the trusted backbone. If even opportunistic anomaly gating proves unreliable in field spoofing tests, disable automatic GNSS fusion in that profile entirely and treat GNSS as a user-confirmed hint.

**Power.** *Kill always-on CV* (already dropped) and, more broadly, if duty-cycled all-sensor draw cannot hold above 24 hours on a 20,000 mAh bank in field testing, cut features until it does. Companion solar is the escape hatch, which is why it is in the MVP.

**EMP.** Not a kill-criterion pillar. Keep it as Faraday-bag insurance guidance only, and resist the temptation to over-invest, but do not let "deprioritize EMP" quietly justify deprioritizing the infrastructure-free mesh and self-powered autonomy, which are exactly what that scenario would need.

# Track deep-dives

## Track A — GPS-denied positioning

**Bottom line.** On a bare commodity phone, GPS-denied positioning is a drift-limited dead-reckoning problem: step counting is effectively solved (~99.5% accuracy [5]), but heading error is the binding constraint and there is no validated multi-kilometer, phone-only benchmark anywhere in the literature [4][27]. Realistic uncorrected accuracy is roughly 0.5-10% of distance travelled over short, controlled, turn-light courses [1][2][3], which extrapolates to tens-to-hundreds of meters of error by the 1-5 km mark, enough to destroy street-level usability without periodic correction. The practical shippable design is phone IMU dead reckoning continuously snapped to a pre-cached OpenStreetMap path graph, with absolute fixes (a slow autonomous GNSS lock, a landmark reset, or peer-shared anchors) whenever they are available; jamming/spoofing and magnetic disturbance near steel and rubble are the dominant failure modes, not sensor noise.

### Shippable now

**Pedestrian dead reckoning (PDR).** Step detection is a solved problem on commodity phones (~99.5% accuracy across low- and high-dynamic pose changes [5]). Position error is dominated by heading, not step counting. The commonly cited band is 0.5-10% of distance travelled [1], and the adversarial verifier confirmed this is a reasonable summary of the literature but stressed it is a *typical spread*, not a hard bound: individual studies range from ~0.14% (short straight indoor runs) to 7.4-12.3% (turn-heavy or unfavorable carry). Well-tuned handheld implementations keep error below ~5% of distance with ~1% stride-length error [2], and a 2023 improved algorithm reports ~1.2-1.6% relative error and ~1.6 m absolute error, but only over 100-200 m fixed indoor courses with 3 subjects [3]. Treat these as good-case, short-to-medium-distance numbers, not guarantees.

**Carry position is a first-order driver, not an implementation detail.** The C-INS/E-PDR study (Wu et al., Sensors 2018) measured 0.9-2.2 m position RMSE but heading RMSE of 2.3 deg handheld rising to 16.6 deg in-pocket across four carry poses [4] (figures verified against the primary source). Independent groups confirm the qualitative ranking (handheld best, pocket/swinging worst) though the magnitude of the gap is algorithm- and study-specific.

**Heading fusion.** Uncorrected gyro-only integration drifts to roughly 90 deg over ~20 minutes; magnetometer + motion-constraint fusion holds it to a few degrees over the same window [6]. Caveat: the verifier marked the specific "90 deg / <4 deg" pair as *contested*. The corrected side is corroborated at "<5 deg after 20 min" [6] and 2.3-5.3 deg RMS for favorable postures [4], but degrades to 10.9-16.6 deg for pocket/swaying [4]; the exact "90 deg" uncorrected figure could not be independently sourced and should be read as order-of-magnitude. The defensible statement: gyro-only becomes unusable within single-digit minutes, and fusion holds single-digit-to-teens of degrees depending on posture and magnetic environment. EKF fusion (accel + gyro + gated magnetometer) is the cheap, real-time backbone already shipping as Android `ROTATION_VECTOR` and iOS CoreMotion.

**Map matching to OSM (the drift eliminator).** This is the mature, commodity-grade half of the stack. HMM/Viterbi and particle-filter matchers snap drifting traces onto plausible OSM edges, with reported error reductions of ~40-97% versus raw PDR/GPS. An outdoor pedestrian system hit 5 m corrected accuracy versus 15-20 m raw GPS under tree cover [7]. Production open-source matchers that already consume OSM and can run fully offline on-device or on a local server include FMM [9] and Valhalla's Meili [10]. Most on-point to this project: a matcher built for phone-to-phone pedestrian map sharing in disaster areas achieved ~5 m median deviation and >85% map accuracy from just 30 minutes of crowd-sourced traces [8]. Particle filters can reach 1.2-2.3 m RMS but need 500-1500 particles, a real battery/CPU cost [30]; a ported HMM+Viterbi is the cheaper choice for a single-user real-time app.

**GNSS with no internet.** Losing A-GPS costs *time*, not steady-state accuracy. Autonomous cold start stretches to ~2-4 minutes in open sky (versus seconds online) because the receiver must demodulate ephemeris (30 s cycle) and almanac (12.5 min cycle) off the signal itself [11]; weak signal makes this much worse. Once locked, open-sky accuracy is ~3-10 m regardless of network [12]. Android's raw GNSS measurements API works fully offline (since Android 7, mandatory on Android 10+) and is the primitive for any on-device correction engine [16]. Important caveat: the sub-meter RTK/PPP/DGNSS results in the literature depend on external correction streams (SBAS, a base station, or IGS orbit/clock products) that normally need internet or a local radio link; a truly offline dual-frequency phone is still meter-level, not centimeter-level [14].

**Barometer (the reliable sensor).** Floor-transition detection hits 95-98% accuracy in multi-story tests [18], and modern MEMS parts resolve sub-meter relative altitude. Only *relative*, same-session altitude is trustworthy (see failure modes).

### Frontier

**Foot-mounted IMU + ZUPT.** Strapping an IMU to the shoe and resetting velocity error each stance phase drives cumulative distance error down to roughly -4% to +2%, with ~0.23 m RMSE over a 100 m closed loop [20] (other low-cost builds report 7.4% straight / 12.3% with turns [29]). This is the accuracy ceiling for hardware-free-ish dead reckoning, but it fundamentally requires a foot-mounted pod, not a phone in a pocket. A cheap companion foot-pod is the most credible near-term hardware bet for this app. Realistic horizon: buildable now as an accessory; not commodity-phone-native.

**ForestBack (2026), the closest published match to this use case.** A breadcrumb-based PDR system for infrastructure-free return navigation reports ~1 m mean RMSE and 1.4 m final-position error, a ~15.8% gain over baseline PDR [21]. But evaluation is indoor, short, and checkpointed; it does not establish a km-scale or multi-hour failure boundary.

**UWB phone-to-phone.** UWB gives the tightest absolute ranging (10-30 cm nominal, <20 cm measured in good conditions) and is already in a large minority of phones (~32.5% of 2025 shipments forecast [23]), so the hardware is in pockets today. Two blockers keep it in the frontier column: iOS and Android UWB stacks do not currently interoperate, and production UWB assumes fixed anchors rather than spontaneous device-to-device ranging [24]; measured accuracy is also unreliable under NLOS/multipath [22]. Realistic horizon: 2-4 years for phone-to-phone group relative positioning, contingent on cross-platform interop landing.

**Wi-Fi RTT (802.11mc/az).** 1-2 m typical, sub-meter best case, on Android 9+, but it needs FTM-capable powered access points that most deployed APs still do not advertise [25]. Essentially useless in the grid-down scenario this project targets. Not a disaster bet.

**Crowdsourced magnetic/radio fingerprinting.** Can reach 0.5-1 m [26], but requires a fingerprint database of the specific area collected *before* the disruption, and all strong numbers are indoor single-building. No evidence it generalizes to open, unbounded outdoor disaster terrain, and disaster damage (collapse, burn) may itself invalidate a pre-collected map. Horizon: viable only for pre-mapped fixed sites with a pre-disruption collection campaign.

**Algorithmic frontier for this app specifically.** Lightweight, disaster-tuned map matchers tolerant of sparse/damaged OSM data and of off-road movement (fire evacuation cutting across open fields, which breaks the classic HMM road-network assumption). Enhanced-HMM work adding an explicit "open-field" hidden state and the disaster-area curve-matcher target exactly this gap [8]. Buildable now; this is engineering, not research risk.

### Hard limits & failure modes

**No validated multi-km phone-only benchmark (the load-bearing caveat).** An independent multi-angle search confirmed that essentially all phone-IMU-only PDR benchmarks are short-range (mostly under 800 m); studies that reach 1 km+ all fuse GNSS, carrier-phase, or visual place recognition to bound drift [27]. The absence is itself informative: nobody trusts uncorrected phone PDR that far. Any multi-km accuracy claim in the product is untested extrapolation. The physics is unforgiving: 1 degree of heading error alone produces ~17.5 m of lateral drift per kilometer [27], so real-world drift with magnetometer interference will likely exceed the ideal 1-2%/distance figures.

**Rubble / steel / vehicles (magnetometer collapse).** This is the sharpest environmental failure. Near rebar, vehicles, and metal, heading error jumps to 30-90 deg in documented extremes versus 5-15 deg under good conditions; even state-of-the-art bias-EKF anomaly rejection only reaches ~17.4 deg RMS in the field [19], not solved. Standard calibration wants a 5 m standoff from ferrous structures, which is often impossible in a collapsed building or parking garage. In exactly the environments this app is for, the compass is least trustworthy, which is why map matching and landmark reset are mandatory, not optional.

**Urban canyon GNSS.** Multipath/NLOS pushes single-frequency error into the tens of meters, with total position error exceeding 50 m in deep canyons and NLOS errors reaching tens to hundreds of meters [13]. Pre-cached 3D-model shadow/sidewalk matching can claw this back under 5 m fully offline [28], but that needs building data cached in advance.

**Barometer, absolute altitude.** Weather-driven ambient pressure shifts are equivalent to 100 m+ of altitude over a few hours, and readings are not cross-calibrated between devices [18]. Only relative, same-session altitude is usable; any crowd-sourced absolute-floor scheme needs per-device bias correction.

**Adversarial jamming/spoofing (civil-unrest/war model).** The most severe and orthogonal failure. Jamming denies a fix outright; spoofing silently returns an arbitrary false position that looks valid. This is now routine in active conflict zones (eastern Mediterranean, Baltic, Middle East) [17]. Design implication: never trust the fused location alone. Run anomaly detection on raw GNSS residuals and corroborate with dead reckoning and peer-shared path/hazard data.

**Smoke / darkness.** IMU, barometer, and magnetometer are unaffected by visibility, so pure PDR degrades gracefully at night or in smoke. The catch: the correction methods that keep PDR usable (visual relocalization, landmark reset, reading street signs) are exactly what smoke and darkness disable, leaving map matching as the only surviving anchor. That raises the reliability bar on the offline OSM graph and on non-visual peer anchors.

**Sparse density.** Crowdsourced map matching and fingerprinting both degrade with few contributors, and the disaster-area matcher's >85% accuracy assumed 30 minutes of accumulated traces [8]. In a thinly populated area or the first minutes of an event, there is not enough peer data to correct drift, and pre-collected fingerprints may already be invalidated by the damage.

**Power (multi-day constraint).** Continuous GNSS is expensive: dual-frequency draws ~37% more power outdoors than single-frequency, and continuous GPS fixing drains a typical phone in roughly 25 hours [15]. For an app expected to run for days without recharge, GNSS must be heavily duty-cycled and the primary continuous mode should be low-power IMU dead reckoning, with GNSS sampled only opportunistically. This reinforces companion solar/battery hardware as part of the real product, not an accessory.

## Track B — CV localization in degraded environments

**Bottom line.** On commodity phones today, the only trustworthy offline CV positioning is *relative*: visual-inertial odometry (ARKit/ARCore) for dead-reckoning plus locally-cached map relocalization (ARWorldMap, Immersal's on-device mode) — all of which drift or fail the moment the scene diverges from what was mapped. Every cloud VPS (Google ARCore Geospatial, Niantic Lightship) is a non-starter without connectivity, which is exactly the condition this project targets. The headline premise — "point your camera and recognize the ruined street from pre-disaster photos" — is **not shippable**: it collapses at night, in smoke, and above all when buildings have moved, and the literature contains essentially zero measured accuracy for the structural-collapse case.

### Shippable now

**Offline relative positioning (VIO)** is the real backbone. ARKit/ARCore visual-inertial odometry needs no network and runs at camera frame rate on stock hardware. Measured drift: ARKit accumulates ~0.14–2.68 m final error over 84 m–3 km walking traverses; ARCore ranges 0.07–3.98 m indoor/outdoor but **fails outright on fast urban-road driving** [7][8]. A corridor test put ARCore at ~0.5 m and ARKit ~1.5 m error after 19 m, with ARKit drift visually noticeable (~10 m accumulated) by ~50 m in one setup [35]. Persistence is offline too: ARKit's ARWorldMap serializes feature points + anchors to a local blob and reloads them to re-align later, no server round trip — but relocalization is not guaranteed if lighting/viewpoint differ [9].

**Immersal is the only commercial VPS with a documented fully-offline on-device path** — spatial maps embedded in the app bundle, matched against the live camera with no cloud or token at runtime. Verifiers rated this **CONFIRMED**, corroborated independently by Magic Leap and Zappar integration docs [1][2]. Two caveats a product team must know: (1) *creating/uploading* maps still needs a cloud token — only runtime localization is offline; (2) the advertised **5–15 cm accuracy is CONTESTED** — that exact figure is single-sourced to a third-party integrator and describes the *cloud* path, another independent source cites a flat 6 cm, and **no offline-path benchmark exists anywhere** [32][33]. Treat sub-15 cm as vendor marketing, not measured offline performance.

**Lightweight VPR for loop closure** is viable on phone-class silicon: CALC, RegionVLAD, CoHOG, and HybridNet run on Raspberry-Pi/Odroid ARM boards at 0.07–34 s/query, 15–30% CPU, and 2–6 W measured draw; CALC was both fastest and most accurate — a plausible shippable-now relocalizer [12].

**Reduced-form geolocation that survives damage:**
- **Skyline-to-DEM** is the most disaster-robust visual cue because terrain doesn't collapse. PeakFinder/PeakVisor already ship phone-camera-to-terrain overlay worldwide (600k–1M+ peaks), proving the render-and-match pipeline works offline on phones [23]. Research shows single-photo mountain-skyline matching placed ~60% of test images within a 10 km² region out of a 10,000 km² search area using 10–30 m DEM, fully offline with **no pre-disaster capture required** [22]. Limitation: needs an exposed horizon — useless in dense-urban or indoor scenes.
- **Semantic/material-segmented urban VPS**: 2.0 m on a high-rise street, 5.5 m in foliage, 15.7 m in a narrow alley in real Hong Kong GNSS-denied tests — but requires a pre-built segmented 3D city model of the *exact* area, a heavy per-city data-prep burden [21].
- **Sequence matching (SeqSLAM class)** is a cheap, shippable mitigation: querying with a short trajectory instead of one frame recovers much of the accuracy lost to extreme change — precision ~1.0, recall up to ~60% under extreme illumination/season change vs near-0% for single-image retrieval [17][18].

**Cloud VPS = non-starters offline (state plainly).** Google ARCore Geospatial requires internet + coarse location to fetch the Street-View-derived tile and matches server-side; no offline mode (verifier **CONFIRMED** from Google's own quickstart). Typical accuracy 5 m / 5°, often ~1 m on iOS — but vendor-reported and only when connected [3][4]. Niantic Lightship is architecturally identical (camera frames to cloud), covers ~140k discrete Wayspots across ~125 cities (point coverage, *not* a navigable mesh), and its on-device mode is a roadmap item, not shipped [5][6]. Both are useless in the exact outage this project addresses.

**Proof the ML backbone can go offline:** Apple's MobileCLIP compresses CLIP-class encoders to 50–173 MB running at 3–15 ms on iPhone-class hardware [26] — evidence the visual backbone under single-image geolocation *can* run on-device, even though no geolocation model ships that way yet.

### Frontier

**Foundation-model VPR (AnyLoc)** is the robustness frontier: DINOv2 ViT-G, 1.1B params, ~314 GFLOPs/image, 85–137 ms on a GPU, and up to 4x higher recall than prior VPR across degraded/unstructured scenes — directly relevant to disaster robustness but **GPU-only today** [13]. Getting it phone-native needs heavy distillation/quantization; MobileNetV3-NetVLAD and Ghost-NetVLAD exist in the literature but without published on-device MB/latency [14]. Realistic horizon for a field-ready distilled robust VPR: **~1–2 years** of real engineering (compression, TFLite/CoreML conversion, on-device benchmarking).

**Phone-native ORB-SLAM3-grade SLAM:** 3–11 cm ATE on EuRoC at 10–40 fps, but on *desktop* CPUs; on Jetson-Nano-class edge hardware the VO core alone drops to ~5 fps at 320 mW, and phone ports are research-only with no published battery/accuracy numbers [10][11]. Battery-tuned multi-map SLAM as a phone product is a **1–3 year stretch**.

**Single-image ML geolocation moving toward phones:** PIGEON gets 44 km median error in-distribution (147–415 km out-of-distribution) — coarse regional only, and weights were **withheld for dual-use reasons** so it can't be redistributed [24]. GeoLocSFT (2025) fine-tuned a 3B VLM on only ~2,700 images to 21% within 25 km — the first result pointing at a phone-quantizable single-image geolocator, though the authors don't test on-device deployment [25]. Combined with MobileCLIP-scale backbones, an offline coarse "which city/valley am I in" model looks plausible in **~1–3 years**, but it will stay km-scale, not street-scale.

**Cross-modal / cross-view fallbacks:** thermal-IR place recognition is the main proposed total-darkness path but faces a severe modality gap (temperature vs reflectance) and thin, non-representative (desert/outdoor) benchmarks. Ground-to-satellite cross-view matching (TransLocNet, 2025) reports >60% position-error reduction over prior SOTA *when given a coarse prior* — promising for re-localizing after a brief GPS outage, but unproven against structurally-altered post-disaster ground truth [30].

**Honest timeline for the headline capability:** no source gives an expert-stated horizon for robust camera localization in combined smoke/rubble/night. Inferred from a 2020–2025 trajectory with no visible inflection point, real robustness is **5–10+ years out — low confidence, a forecast not a settled fact** [27].

### Hard limits & failure modes

**Night.** The best hierarchical method on Aachen Day-Night drops from **84.8% → 43.9%** of queries localized at fine precision (0.25 m/2°) *purely* from the day→night appearance change; pure image-retrieval (DenseVLAD, NetVLAD alone, FAB-MAP) hits **0.0%** at fine precision at night, and even an oracle-assisted baseline fails ~20% of night queries outright. On RobotCar, night+rain retrieval falls to ~10% coarse accuracy vs ~85–95% by day [17].

**Vegetation/seasonal change.** Extended CMU "park" (heavy foliage) collapses structure-based localization to ~8.9% at fine precision — as bad as or worse than night, proving that scene-*content* change (not just lighting) independently breaks localization [17].

**Smoke/haze.** No source quantifies VPR/6DOF recall under smoke — a genuine evidence gap. Mechanistically, smoke/dust inject strong non-repeatable gradients and suppress contrast, so feature counts collapse and tracking fails **abruptly (a cliff, not a slope)** rather than degrading gracefully [20][31].

**Structural collapse — the core premise, and the weakest point.** There is essentially *no* published measurement of visual relocalization against a pre-collapse reference once buildings are actually down; the entire building-damage literature (xBD, BRIGHT) is overhead satellite classification, not ground-level relocalization [19]. Post-earthquake CV work states debris matching is "difficult or impossible" with vision alone [31]. Collapse violates the static-structure assumption every VPR/SLAM method depends on, so performance should be expected *at least* as bad as the worst appearance-change regimes and likely worse — but this is **inference, not measured data**. Immersal's own matching degrades against rubble/smoke/fire that alter pre-disaster appearance [1]. The literal "recognize the ruins from stored photos" feature is not supported by evidence.

**Strongest real-world signal.** DARPA SubT (dust, smoke, rubble, darkness, no GPS) — the closest testbed to this threat model — was won by CERBERUS using LiDAR + multi-modal multi-robot fusion; **camera-only localization was not trusted as the primary channel even with an unlimited hardware budget** [27]. Even curated night/occlusion VPR benchmarks top out at 75.5–84.2% reliability with 26–35 m top-1 errors near the correctness threshold [28], and 2024–25 surveys report learned descriptors (SuperPoint, R2D2, LightGlue) "deteriorate significantly" under extreme conditions [29].

**Adversarial (project threat model).** The CV findings do not directly cover adversarial poisoning of visual maps — flag this as an evidence gap. The nearest analog is benign scene-divergence, which already breaks matching; a *deliberately* altered scene or a poisoned crowd-sourced reference map would be strictly easier for an adversary than the natural failures above. Any offline reference database must be treated as attackable.

**Power cost.** No rigorous phone-specific VIO mAh/hour figure exists — another evidence gap. Proxies: general camera-heavy AR draws ~10–15%/hour (up to 3x a normal app), one VIO-only anecdote ~6%/hour [15][16]; lightweight VPR adds 2–6 W on ARM-class silicon [12]; ORB-SLAM3's VO core alone is 320 mW on Jetson-Nano-class hardware [11]. Continuous visual localization is a meaningful drain vs idle GNSS — a real constraint for multi-hour disaster use and a reason to **duty-cycle vision** rather than run it continuously.

**Design implication.** The viable minimal form is **landmark-anchored, human-in-the-loop matching**: cache low-bandwidth photos of large stable structures (skyline, major buildings, street topology), do coarse automatic matching, and let the user make the final call. This mirrors how humans actually navigate disaster zones — coarse cognitive maps and gross salient structures survive when fine recognition fails [34].

## Track C — Mesh networking + adversarial trust

**Bottom line.** Phone-to-phone offline messaging is shippable today, but only over BLE, and only as a foreground, screen-on app: the binding constraint is not radio physics but iOS/Android background-execution policy plus short per-hop range (roughly 10-100 m). Every phone-only mesh app audited adversarially has failed (Bridgefy was fully broken: deanonymization, impersonation, and whole-network takedown from one crafted message), and no serverless scheme is Sybil-proof; the honest goal is raising attacker cost, not eliminating attacks. LoRa/Meshtastic companion hardware ($20-50) buys an order-of-magnitude more range and decouples the network from crowd density, at the price of ~1 kbps throughput and a 237-byte packet ceiling that forces tiny, structured hazard messages.

### Shippable now

**BLE is the only cross-platform lingua franca.** Wi-Fi Aware (NAN) is Android-only with no public iOS API, and iOS peer-to-peer Wi-Fi runs over the private, undocumented AWDL layer under Multipeer Connectivity [7]. So any iOS↔Android mesh must fall back to BLE GATT or L2CAP, which caps usable throughput well below Wi-Fi. Practical per-hop range is 10-100 m depending on obstacles; the ~100 m / 330 ft figure is open-line-of-sight marketing, and multiple engineering sources put typical real-world indoor range closer to 10-30 m [1]. Consumer apps (Bridgefy, and Jack Dorsey's 2025 BitChat) already demonstrate pure-BLE mesh relay with no internet, cellular, or account, using TTL relay (BitChat: 7 hops) to extend coverage across a crowd [1].

**Throughput, honestly (verifiers downgraded the raw research here).**
- GATT: the "~700 kbps on 1M PHY" figure is a best-case lab number on dev boards. Real phone-to-phone GATT is more like 300-400 kbps once ATT/L2CAP overhead and OS-controlled connection intervals are counted, and iOS gives the app no control over connection interval [2]. Conversely, the research claim that 2M PHY stays "well under 1 Mbps" is wrong: bench data shows 2M-PHY GATT reaching ~1.0-1.4 Mbps with DLE and a 247-byte MTU [3].
- L2CAP connection-oriented channels: Apple's own WWDC demo shows ~394 kbps as a ceiling (L2CAP + Extended Data Length + 15 ms interval), but real iPhone-to-iPhone 1 MB transfers measured only ~37-138 kbps, and Android fragments L2CAP packets unless a large ATT MTU is separately negotiated [4]. Budget tens of kbps sustained on mixed/older devices.
- Extended advertising (BLE 5.0+): connectionless broadcast of up to 255 bytes/packet, chainable to ~1,650 bytes, with no GATT connection [5]. This is the right primitive for lightweight "danger here" hazard beacons (unacknowledged, best-effort), not bulk transfer.
- Wi-Fi Direct: ~19-23 Mbps single-hop (an order of magnitude above BLE), but it degrades steeply with hops. The often-cited 19/18/10 Mbps triple is unverified; the primary Android testbed actually measured roughly a halving by the second hop (~19 → ~8.4 → ~5.0 Mbps) because every relay contends for the same airtime [6]. And Wi-Fi Direct/Aware do not interoperate iOS↔Android, so it cannot be the cross-platform baseline.

**LoRa / Meshtastic as companion hardware.** Mature and cheap: Heltec V3 ~$25, LILYGO T-Beam ~$25-50, RAK WisBlock kits ~$25-37, paired to iOS/Android over BLE via the official app with a built-in map view [10]. Range is bimodal: dense urban commonly a few hundred meters to ~1-2 km per hop (a suburban-valley tester saw zero nodes over 24 h), versus 10-80 km rural line-of-sight, with 166-331 km record stunts [8][9]. The default LongFast preset runs ~1.07 kbps (presets span ~0.18-21.9 kbps), and packets are hard-capped at 256 bytes (~237 usable) [9] — well matched to a compressed lat/lon + hazard-type + timestamp record, useless for map tiles (those must be pre-loaded and only referenced/diffed over the mesh).

**Trust and merge primitives that already exist.**
- Meshtastic uses Trust-On-First-Use key binding, and firmware 2.8.x added XEdDSA packet signing so identity/report packets can be cryptographically signed rather than trusted on bare TOFU — a low-overhead pattern to copy [11].
- Corroboration and decay are field-proven: Waze deletes a report after two consecutive "not there" votes and auto-expires hazards after ~30 min [12]; Ushahidi/Standby Task Force converged on an N≥3 independent-source rule after the Ebola misinformation problem [13].
- Rangzen computes trust with no PKI and no author identity, via Private Set Intersection over mutual-friend lists at each encounter; on real 2013-era Android hardware the full exchange ran ~6.6 s median (100 messages/30 friends) at 5.5%/hr battery in worst-case continuous mode [14].
- Conflict-free map merge: delta-CRDT libraries are usable today — Automerge 2.0's columnar binary format is ~1 byte/character overhead (vs >1,300 bytes/char in naive JSON) [15], Loro reports 2-5x smaller encodings than Yjs/Automerge [16], and the commercial Ditto SDK does field-level delta sync over BLE/Wi-Fi/LAN with no server [17]. Keep each annotation edit well under 237 bytes so it fits one LoRa packet.

### Frontier

- **Secure group mesh (12-48 months).** "Amigo" (ACM CCS 2025) pairs continuous group-key agreement with a mesh routing protocol tuned for realistic protest mobility (link churn, spectrum contention), directly answering the Bridgefy-break paper's finding that no shipped app serves security and reliability at once [18]. It is a peer-reviewed prototype, not a product; the Signal-adoption precedent (Bridgefy took ~1 year post-disclosure just to add baseline E2E crypto, still with no public post-fix audit) puts a hardened, phone-deployable equivalent 2-4+ years out [24][18].
- **BLE 5.4 PAwR and Coded/2M PHY tradeoffs** remain immature in chipset/OS support as of 2025-2026; not yet a safe cross-device baseline.
- **Spatial delta-CRDTs** (geohash-partitioned so peers only sync cells they are physically near) plus signed/authenticated deltas would cut both message count and tombstone-GC pressure while addressing data poisoning. No off-the-shelf library exists; ~12-24 months if purpose-built.
- **Serverless Sybil defenses** — RF-fingerprinting (the Mason Test) and proximity-attestation via Wi-Fi collocation challenges — are early-stage research prototypes, sensitive to propagation conditions, not shippable [12].

### Hard limits & failure modes

**OS background execution is the single biggest real-world blocker.** iOS Multipeer Connectivity stops browsing/advertising within about a second of backgrounding, so a phone stops relaying the moment its screen locks [19]; Core Bluetooth throttles peripheral advertising in the background and can terminate the app to reclaim memory, so background BLE relay is best-effort only [20]. Android silently throttles unfiltered scans after 30 s, reports zero advertising packets in Doze, and post-Oreo requires a foreground service with a permanent user-visible notification to scan continuously [21]. Net design consequence: assume the app is in the foreground, screen on, whenever it is actively relaying — background modes are a partial mitigation, not a fix.

**Sparse density / night / dispersed crowds.** Opportunistic DTN delivery over human-carried nodes needs either near-continuous BLE-range chains (protest density ~0.5 people/m²) or node mobility to substitute for density: directed continuum-percolation theory puts the critical mean node degree around ν≈6.08 for a mobile DTN (vs ~4.51 for a static live mesh) [22]. Real adoption undercuts the headlines — FireChat's 2014 Hong Kong "mesh" fame was mostly app-store downloads; a fact-check found only one credible report of actual offline mesh use among dozens of articles [23]. At night or in dispersed rubble/evacuation conditions, the human node graph thins exactly when you need it, and mass one-way evacuation mobility is worst-case for resource-efficient predictive routing, forcing back toward expensive epidemic flooding.

**Smoke / rubble / obstruction (RF, not light).** Radios don't care about darkness, but they care intensely about obstruction and terrain. Urban Meshtastic can collapse to a few hundred meters or fail entirely (0 nodes/24 h in a suburban valley) [8]; dense wet forest drops LoRa to ~0.4 km; concrete and metal roughly halve BLE range [1]. Antenna height matters more than any radio setting (3-10x range swings), which a phone in a pocket at street level cannot exploit.

**Adversarial (the track's core threat).** Bridgefy — the app actually used across 2019-2020 Hong Kong, India, Iran, Belarus, Thailand, and US BLM protests — was shown to permit persistent BLE user tracking, trivial impersonation (no authentication), broken confidentiality (a working padding-oracle attack fully decrypting a message in well under 14 hours), and total network takedown from a single "zip bomb" message that crashes every client and requires reinstall [24]. Sybil attacks are cheap wherever proof-of-physical-presence is absent: Ghost Riders registered 1,000 fake Waze accounts in a day and ran 1,000 virtual-vehicle Sybils on one commodity Linux box, sustaining fabricated hazards that rerouted real traffic [12]. Social-graph friction (Rangzen) only helps because forming trusted fake edges requires physically meeting real users — even then an idealized state-level attacker degraded honest reach by 30% over 48 h [14]. No scheme here is Sybil-proof without a central authority; treat every hazard report as unauthenticated until signed and corroborated (N≥3), and prefer TTL-based hard expiry over relying on causal-stability garbage collection, since OR-Set/OR-Map tombstones grow unbounded in a partitioned phone mesh that may never reach causal stability [27].

**Power cost.** Wi-Fi Aware draws significantly more than BLE and its low-latency "instant communication" mode auto-disables after 30 s to protect battery [7]. Continuous BLE mesh is costly on phones — Briar drains roughly 4x a normal messenger from constant scanning [25] — and Android's mandatory foreground-service notification is a standing UX/battery tax. On LoRa hardware, ESP32-class boards (Heltec, T-Beam) last only tens of hours while nRF52-class boards (RAK WisBlock) run days to a week, with GPS the dominant drain [10]. Regulatory duty-cycle limits are a hard ceiling independent of battery: under the US 1% duty cycle a 16-byte packet can be sent only ~100 times/hour per node, so hazard-map gossip must be tiny, batched, and prioritized — never a continuous delta stream [26].

## Track D — Hardware, power, EMP & provisioning

**Bottom line.** On commodity phones today, offline maps plus routing and multi-day power autonomy are essentially solved at region scale, and the dominant battery cost is the screen and camera/CV, not GNSS or BLE. The genuinely hard, unsolved problems are adversarial: GNSS spoofing already degrades civilian phones over multi-country regions in live conflicts, and the software-only detections that work on a connected phone fail in exactly the offline/low-connectivity conditions this app targets. EMP is a real physics threat to infrastructure but a low priority for a handheld, because a surviving phone would have nothing to talk to.

### Shippable now

**Power budget (measured, with caveats).** The best-anchored figure is per-fix energy: 318 mJ/fix on a Xiaomi Mi 8 (dual-frequency GNSS) and 232 mJ/fix on a Redmi Note 7 (single-frequency), which at a 1 Hz fix rate is roughly 232-320 mW of continuous system-level draw [1]. Verifiers confirmed this figure but flag that it is one 2019 study on two specific phones, not an industry constant. It sits well above bare-chip datasheet numbers (modern u-blox M10/F11 tracking chips run 7-25 mW [2]) because system-level draw adds the RF front end, baseband, and application-processor work. The widely quoted "176 mW GPS / 800 mW cellular / 1000 mW camera" breakdown [3] should NOT be treated as validated constants: verifiers traced all three to a single book (Vallina-Rodriguez and Crowcroft) popularized by one blog post, and independent circuit-level work (Carroll and Heiser, GPS module 143-166 mW) [4] corroborates only the order of magnitude and the ranking (camera > radio > GPS), not the exact values.

**Runtime.** Summing subsystems (lit map screen 0.5-1.5 W, GNSS 0.2-0.3 W, aggressive BLE scan 0.1-0.3 W, camera viewfinder about 1 W, CV inference adding 0.1 W to multiple W) gives an all-on disaster-nav draw of roughly 1.5-3.5 W, which on a typical 15-20 Wh battery (4,000-5,500 mAh) [5] is about 4-13 hours with no duty-cycling. That is a back-of-envelope synthesis, not a measured benchmark. The most credible real-world measurement is a 2026 Android Police test on a Pixel 10 Pro Fold: standard turn-by-turn nav drew 8%/hr in light mode (about 12.5 h), 5%/hr in dark mode (about 20 h), and 3%/hr in Google Maps' new Power Saving mode (about 33 h) [6]. This contradicts the "4-6 h intensive" figure circulating on SEO blogs, which verifiers flagged as citogenesis with no traceable methodology; do not budget against it.

**Duty-cycling is the highest-leverage lever and ships today.** Android's SCAN_MODE_LOW_POWER scans 0.5 s then sleeps 4.5 s (about a 10% duty cycle) [7], cutting BLE draw by roughly 60% versus continuous low-latency scanning in the one (dated, 2013 Nexus 4) direct measurement available [8]. GNSS fix-rate throttling (1 Hz down to one fix per 30-60 s) cuts that contribution roughly 10x; sampling CV every few seconds instead of every frame and routing inference through the NPU rather than the GPU cuts that subsystem 80-95%. Implementation caveat verifiers surfaced: Android auto-downgrades any app to SCAN_MODE_OPPORTUNISTIC after about 30 minutes of continuous scanning, and a foreground service does NOT exempt you (that claim is unsupported), so plan to stop and restart scans well under 30 minutes [9]. Treat the specific BLE current figures (30-90 mA continuous, 37 mA duty-cycled) as decade-old single-device anecdata [8]: the pattern holds, the constants do not.

**Field charging and autonomy.** Portable solar realizes only about 50-70% of nameplate in good sun and far less under cloud. The strongest single data point is an OutdoorGearLab bench test of a 10 W folding panel producing about 7 W-equivalent in direct alpine sun but only about 1.5 W under simulated overcast, a 79% falloff [10]. A 100 W panel yields roughly 300-600 Wh/day across 3-6 peak-sun-hours [11]. Hand-crank chargers are emergency-only: about 15-25 W of human mechanical input nets only about 2.5-5 W to the phone after conversion losses, so a full charge needs roughly 45-75 minutes of vigorous cranking (about 1-3% battery per minute) [12]. Power banks lose 15-35% of nameplate to conversion: a 20,000 mAh (about 74 Wh nameplate) bank delivers about 55-60 Wh usable, roughly 3-4 phone charges [13], and clears the 100 Wh airline carry-on cap [14]. Net realistic autonomy for phone nav plus a low-power mesh relay is about 3-5 days on one large power bank, and effectively indefinite with even a 10-20 W folding panel, since a Meshtastic relay idles at only about 8-12 mA (30-45 mW) and documented solar setups oversupply node draw by 6-10x [15].

**Offline maps and routing.** Solved at country/region scale. Organic Maps stores a country as a compact .mwm file, roughly 100-800 MB (Portugal about 250 MB, Morocco about 400 MB), and about 8.3 GB for the full USA [16]. OSMAnd's global routing-only dataset is about 800 MB; a mid-size region is about 200 MB full or about 80 MB roads-only [17]. Routing graphs scale with the extract: Valhalla runs about 100 MB for a small country up to about 10 GB for the continental US [18], and a GraphHopper graph is roughly 0.6-1.2x the source extract [19]. A realistic disaster kit (home region plus neighbors, vector basemap plus national routing graph, no raster imagery) totals low single-digit GB, trivially inside 64-512 GB phones. The pre-download UX (per-region size shown, Wi-Fi-only gating, auto-eviction, an Apple Maps download cap of about 5 GB) is mature and should be copied, not reinvented [20]. Two hazards: raster/satellite imagery explodes size (one city is about 15 MB as vector but up to 800 MB as raster at zoom 17 [21]), so cap zoom or default to vector; and whole-planet is a non-starter on-device (planet .pbf about 64 GB [22], Protomaps PMTiles zoom 0-15 about 120 GB [23], an OSRM planet contraction-hierarchy graph of about 230 GiB needing about 123 GiB RAM [24]). Ship pre-cut regional packages, never a global blob.

**GNSS-jamming: software mitigations that ship today.** Multi-constellation/multi-frequency (GPS, Galileo, GLONASS, BeiDou; L1/L5) is already standard in commodity SoCs and helps against single-band or single-constellation attacks [25]. A published Android method (NAVIGATION/ION journal) detects jamming versus spoofing purely in software using four on-device signals: GNSS-versus-network location divergence (100 m threshold), the OS isFromMockProvider() flag, GNSS-versus-system-clock mismatch, and AGC/C-N0 drops (greater than 5 dB) [26]. The zero-cost fix Ukrainian EW specialists recommend, disabling automatic network/GNSS timezone updates and never trusting GNSS-derived time for critical logic, should be a default [27]. Pedestrian dead-reckoning off the phone IMU holds cumulative error to roughly 0.6-5% of distance traveled in published studies (best case 0.5 m after 800 m walked) [28], a usable on-foot GPS-outage bridge, though it is not commercialized as a seamless fallback in mainstream map apps.

**Faraday-bag insurance.** Storing one spare phone or power bank in an independently lab-tested (MIL-STD-188-125-2) Faraday bag is cheap real insurance: good bags achieve 60-105+ dB attenuation versus about 7 dB for cheap mylar "signal-blocking" pouches [29][30]. Do not market anything as "EMP-proof."

### Frontier

**Crowdsourced multi-phone jamming detection/localization (12-24 months).** A US firm (Zephr) is field-testing networked stock Android Pixels in Ukraine that use raw GNSS chip measurements (AGC, Doppler, carrier phase, code phase) across many phones as a "distributed antenna" to detect and triangulate jammers without relying on GPS for the detection itself [31]. This is the most promising near-term direction because it needs no new hardware, only software plus a mesh to share observations, which is directly aligned with this app's phone-to-phone model.

**Cryptographic GNSS authentication (multiple years).** Galileo OSNMA (navigation-message authentication) and GPS Chimera are the real structural fix for spoofing, but neither is standard on commodity handsets yet and this track did not surface a primary source benchmarking phone-chipset support, so treat the horizon as multi-year and the confidence as low until measured.

**Alt-PNT with chip-scale atomic clocks (years).** A CSAC plus MEMS IMU and last-known fix can hold sub-microsecond timing for hours after GNSS loss [32], but this is military/high-end silicon today, not in phone BOMs; it is a plausible companion-hardware or future-phone feature, not shippable now.

**NPU-routed always-on CV (1-2 years, maturing).** Dedicated ultra-low-power NPUs already run YOLO-class detection at 11-80 mW in lab silicon (MAX78000, NXP MCXN947), and phone NPUs (Apple Neural Engine, Qualcomm Hexagon) deliver GPU-class throughput at single-digit watts [33]. The lever is routing continuous CV through the NPU rather than the GPU, but phone NPUs are not yet publicly benchmarked at the same rigor, so the power-affordability of always-on CV is directionally supported, not proven.

**Solar mesh nodes are basically here.** A 5 W panel plus a 50 Wh battery runs a Meshtastic relay for about a year [15]; the remaining frontier is ruggedization, provisioning, and trust, not the electronics.

### Hard limits and failure modes

**Power: the killer is concurrency.** GNSS and BLE are minor draws; the screen (0.5-2.5 W, with bright outdoor map reading at the top of that range [3]) and camera-plus-CV are what drain the battery. Camera viewfinder alone is about 1 W (a dated, verifier-contested figure [3]), and concurrent on-device CV inference pushes a modern SoC to multiple watts, so an always-on "point the camera to detect hazards" mode is a battery-killer measured in a few hours, not days. It must be strictly duty-cycled or user-triggered.

**Smoke, night, rubble.** Camera-CV degrades or fails in smoke, darkness, and dust (low confidence, not directly measured in this track but physically expected) exactly when hazards are worst, and thermal or other sensors are not present on commodity phones. GNSS in urban canyon, forest, or indoors both loses fixes and, when it retries, drains about 38% faster than in strong signal (versus about 13%) [34]: this differential is the one peer-reviewed figure that survived verification, while the round runtime numbers around it did not. Under rubble or indoors with no GNSS, PDR is the only fallback, and its heading drift (magnetometer disturbance near steel/rebar, gyro bias) is the dominant unbounded error source [28].

**Adversarial: this is where it breaks worst.** Matched-power spoofing in low- or no-connectivity environments, the app's core operating condition, is exactly what the software-only Android detection cannot catch, because the network-location cross-check requires connectivity [26]. Multi-constellation resilience is defeated by a sufficiently powerful multi-band spoofer broadcasting fake signals on all civil bands at once, as seen in the Israel case using "many times traditional power" [35]. Live-conflict footprints are large: Ukrainian anti-drone spoofing displaced civilian phones to Russian cities and shifted timezones city-wide for weeks [27]; Israeli spoofing affected 7 countries with roughly 2,000 spoofed aircraft in a 72-hour window and forced a Turkish Airlines flight to abort its Beirut approach and circle about 40 minutes [36][37]; aviation spoofing rose about 500% year-over-year to a peak of roughly 1,500 flights per day [38]. And in a phone-to-phone mesh with no central server, data poisoning has no natural arbiter: trust and filtering are an unsolved design problem, not a config toggle.

**Sparse density and hardware limits.** A phone's integrated BLE cannot run for weeks like a dedicated node; standalone mesh autonomy is bounded (an 800 mAh node at about 8 mA lasts only about 4 days without solar [15]). Hand-crank is not a nav power source (about 2.5-5 W, an emergency call/text lever only [12]). Solar collapses under cloud (79% falloff [10]), and an undersized panel with no MPPT controller caused real-world winter node failure [15], so sizing margin matters more than headline watts.

**EMP (stretch threat, deprioritize).** The physics holds: E1 HEMP (50 kV/m peak, about 2.5 ns rise, 23 ns wide, 0.115 J/m^2 total energy [39]) damages electronics mainly by coupling into long conductors (power lines, cables, long antennas), so a phone that is off, unplugged, and not cabled is the least-vulnerable class [39][40]. But "not cabled" is not "immune": 50 kV/m can induce roughly 5,000 V on a bare 10 cm trace or antenna stub, miniaturized ICs run at a few volts [39], and a phone plugged into a charger at the moment of burst moves into the high-risk category. The best public empirical benchmark is EMP Commission vehicle testing (about 1 in 50 older 1987-2002 vehicles disabled [41]); there is NO public direct HEMP test of a modern pocketed smartphone [39], so phone survival is inferred, not measured. The decisive point for product priority: even a surviving handset is useless because cell towers, backhaul, and grid power (all long-line infrastructure) are the primary HEMP casualties and would be down regardless [42]. Treat EMP as low-priority relative to the natural-disaster and civil-unrest cases.

# Appendix — Adversarial verification log

Each load-bearing quantitative claim below was checked by an independent agent instructed to refute it with separate sources. `confirmed` = multiple independent sources agree; `contested` = real but conditional / sources disagree; `refuted` = contradicted (none this run).

### Track A — GPS-denied positioning

Android support is independently confirmed to be inconsistent: Nordic DevZone reports Android phones (e.g. Pixel 4) negotiating Extended Data Length to 251 bytes but still fragmenting L2CAP CoC packets down to 27 octets unless a large ATT MTU is separately requested, and Android ties L2CAP packet size to ATT MTU negotiation in a way iOS does not - meaning the 394 kbps figure is essentially an iOS-side, favorable-condition number and Android throughput/adoption is measurably worse and more device-dependent. Net: the number and its 'ceiling, not typical' framing are correct and well-supported; product teams should treat 394 kbps as an optimistic upper bound achievable only on paired-up-to-date iOS devices with a fast (15ms) connection interval, and budget for real sustained throughput in the tens of kbps on mixed/older devices, with Android requiring per-device validation.
- **[CONFIRMED]** BLE extended advertising (Bluetooth 5.0+) allows connectionless broadcast of up to 255 bytes per secondary-channel packet, chainable to ~1,650 bytes total, without ever forming a GATT connection — useful for lightweight hazard-beacon broadcast (e.g. 'danger here' pings) rather than bulk file transfer. _(255 B/packet, ~1,650 B chained)_
  - The 255 B/packet and ~1,650 B chained-total figures are correct and corroborated independently by Bluetooth SIG's own blog, Nordic Semiconductor, Silicon Labs docs, and an Espressif/Nordic engineering thread — this is standard BLE 5.0 Core Spec behavior (AUX_ADV_IND up to 255 B, chained via AUX_CHAIN_IND to ~1,650 B for non-connectable advertising; connectable extended advertising caps lower, around 191 B). Real-world caveats for a hazard-beacon design: (1) this data moves connectionless and one-way — true, so it avoids GATT connection overhead, but delivery is unacknowledged/best-effort (no retransmission, no ordering guarantee), so it's fine for periodic "danger here" pings but not for anything requiring confirmed delivery; (2) chip/OS support for scanning full extended advertisements is uneven — iOS support depends on device hardware and exposes it via `CBCentralManager.Feature.extendedScanAndConnect`, and background scanning on iOS coalesces duplicate advertisements and slows scan interval, degrading real-time hazard-alert latency; (3) actual usable payload for an app is smaller than 1,650 B once BLE advertising-data framing (AD structure headers, flags, UUIDs) is subtracted; (4) range/throughput at the phy layer for extended advertising isn't dramatically different from legacy BLE (still tens of meters typical indoor/outdoor line-of-sight dependent) — the SIG/vendor sources describe payload capacity, not an independent range or throughput benchmark, so those numbers still need separate verification from RF-range studies.
- **[CONTESTED]** Wi-Fi Direct throughput in multi-hop chains measured around 19 Mbps at 1 hop, 18 Mbps at 2 hops, and 10 Mbps at 3 hops — an order of magnitude above BLE, but degrading with hop count because each relay competes for the same channel/airtime. _(~19 Mbps (1 hop) down to ~10 Mbps (3 hops))_
  - The qualitative claim — Wi-Fi Direct throughput sits roughly an order of magnitude above BLE and degrades with hop count because relays share the same channel/airtime — is well corroborated. I directly read the full text of Casetti et al. ("Content-centric Routing in Wi-Fi Direct Multi-group Networks," arXiv:1412.0880, also published IJIRMF 2016), a real Android testbed study, which measured application-layer throughput of ~19 Mbit/s for a 2-device/1-group (~1-hop) link, ~8.4 Mbit/s for 3-device/1-group (~2-hop relay), and ~5.0 Mbit/s for 4-device/2-group (~3-hop relay) — plus separate broadcast-path measurements of 4.6 and 2.5 Mbit/s. A second, independently reported study (via search summary, unverified primary text) gives 22.9/10.6/6.2 Mbit/s for 1/2/3 hops. Both real sources show the SAME 1-hop ballpark (~19-23 Mbps, matching the claim) but a materially STEEPER drop between hop 1 and hop 2 (roughly halving or worse) than the claim's "19→18" (near-flat). I could not locate or verify a primary source anywhere that actually reports the claim's exact 19/18/10 triple — the MDPI V2P paper (Almeida et al., JSAN 2020, doi:10.3390/jsan9020028) that search summaries kept attributing this figure to is behind a 403 paywall and, per its own abstract/topic, is about vehicle-to-pedestrian range/PDR, not a hop-count throughput chain — so that attribution looks like a search-summarization artifact, not a verified fact. BLE real-world throughput independently checks out far lower (~0.3-2 Mbps depending on PHY/MTU), so the "order of magnitude above BLE" framing holds regardless of which hop-count numbers you use. Bottom line for a product team: trust the mechanism and the ~19-23 Mbps single-hop figure and the general "meaningful mid-chain drop by 2-3 hops" pattern, but do NOT cite the specific 19/18/10 figures as a validated number — real measurements suggest steeper degradation (more like halving) between hop 1 and hop 2, and all these numbers come from small controlled Android testbeds (2-6 devices, short range, non-rooted phones), not field conditions with interference, mobility, or contention from other traffic.

### Track D — Hardware/power/EMP

- **[CONTESTED]** At the whole-smartphone system level (RF front end + baseband + application processor work), continuous GPS draw is much higher than bare-chip figures: one widely-cited smartphone power breakdown puts GPS at ~176 mW, comparable to or below an active cellular radio (~800 mW) and camera viewfinder (~1000 mW). _(~176 mW (system-level, one phone))_
  - The specific numbers (~176 mW GPS, ~800 mW active cellular, ~1000 mW camera viewfinder) all trace to a single lineage: the Cambridge University Press book "Smartphone Energy Consumption: Modeling and Optimization" (Vallina-Rodriguez and Crowcroft), popularized verbatim by Pete Warden's 2015 blog post -- not three independently replicated measurements. Independent hardware-instrumented studies partially support the general order of magnitude and ranking (camera/display-class draws > active radio > GPS) but disagree on the exact figures: Carroll and Heiser's circuit-level measurement (USENIX ATC 2010, Openmoko Freerunner) found the GPS module alone drawing 143-166 mW, close to the 176 mW figure, but that device had no camera or 3G modem, so it cannot corroborate the cellular/camera comparison. Other surveys report 3G/cellular transceiver draw as low as 355-632 mW (well below the claimed ~800 mW) and bare GPS chipsets as low as 40 mW at the component level versus ~176-185 mW system-level, showing GPS/cellular power varies 5-10x depending on whether you measure the bare RF chip or whole-phone system draw, and on network generation (2.5G/3G/4G), signal strength, and antenna state. For product planning: treat "GPS system-level draw is roughly 150-200 mW, below active-radio and well below camera-viewfinder draw" as a reasonably safe order-of-magnitude claim, but do not cite 176/800/1000 mW as precise, broadly validated constants -- they are one author's rough estimates from one secondary source, and real measured values swing widely by device generation, network mode, and measurement point (chip vs. system).
- **[CONFIRMED]** Direct measurements on real phones performing a GNSS position fix show energy costs of ~318 mJ (Xiaomi Mi 8, dual-frequency GNSS) and ~232 mJ (Redmi Note 7, single-frequency) per fix; at a 1 Hz fix rate this is roughly equivalent to 232-320 mW of continuous system-level power, i.e. in the same ballpark as the 176 mW figure above and well above bare-chip datasheet numbers. _(232-320 mW equivalent (1 Hz fixes, real phones))_
  - The core mJ/fix figures (318 mJ ± 32 for Xiaomi Mi 8 dual-frequency, 232 mJ ± 20 for Redmi Note 7 single-frequency) trace to one specific peer-reviewed source — Karki & Won, "Characterizing Power Consumption of Dual-Frequency GNSS of a Smartphone" (arXiv:1910.13041, also published IEEE Xplore 9322317) — and every hit I found (arXiv, IEEE, ResearchGate, Semantic Scholar) is citing/echoing that same underlying study rather than an independent remeasurement, so this is technically single-source for the exact numbers, not multi-source replication. That said: (1) the math translating mJ/fix at 1 Hz into equivalent continuous mW is trivially correct (1 mJ/s = 1 mW); (2) the qualitative pattern — dual-frequency costing ~30-40% more than single-frequency — is physically expected (two RF front-ends) and unsurprising; (3) independent, unrelated measurement studies of system-level GPS power on other phones land in a comparable-order-of-magnitude range (Galaxy S II ~771 mW to acquire, one study ~185 mW at fixed position, another ~425 mW duty-cycled), so 232-320 mW is plausible but sits inside a genuinely wide literature spread (roughly 120-770+ mW depending on device generation, chipset, and test protocol) rather than a tightly convergent figure; and (4) bare chipset datasheets are indeed much lower (u-blox notes single-band tracking chips at ~120 mW in 2010, ~70 mW by 2015, ~25 mW today), corroborating the "well above datasheet" claim. Net: figure is credible and internally consistent, but a product team should treat 232-320 mW as one data point from one 2019 study on two specific 2018-19 Xiaomi phones (Broadcom BCM47755 dual-freq vs Snapdragon 660 integrated single-freq GNSS), not a settled industry-wide constant — real-world GNSS system power will vary substantially by chipset generation, assisted-GNSS vs cold-start conditions, and indoor/outdoor signal environment.
- **[CONTESTED]** Real-world GPS navigation use is reported to drain a phone in roughly 8-12 hours under standard settings, dropping to 4-6 hours under intensive/high-accuracy mapping mode; weak-signal environments (dense urban canyon, forest canopy, indoors) increase drain substantially (~38% higher drain reported vs ~13% in strong-signal areas) because the receiver works harder / retries more. _(8-12 h standard, 4-6 h intensive; weak signal +~2-3x drain rate)_
  - Two separate sub-claims here need separating. (1) The 13% vs 38% weak-signal-vs-strong-signal drain differential traces to a real peer-reviewed source, Tawalbeh et al., "Greener and Smarter Phones for Future Cities: Characterizing the Impact of GPS Signal Strength on Power Consumption," IEEE Access 4 (2016): 858-868 - this is corroborated and mechanistically sound (weak SNR forces longer acquisition/re-acquisition and more correlator search, which draws more current), though I could not pull the full text to confirm the exact test device/duration, so treat the multiplier as approximate rather than a universal constant. (2) The "8-12h standard / 4-6h intensive" runtime figures do NOT have a traceable primary source - they appear verbatim or near-verbatim across a cluster of low-quality SEO blogs (gpslab.in, timeero, hubstaff, carlock, backpackingguys) with no cited methodology, device, battery capacity, or screen-brightness condition, which is a citogenesis red flag rather than independent corroboration. Actual measured counter-evidence I found points the other way: a 2026 Android Police real-world test on a Pixel 10 Pro Fold measured only 8%/hour battery drain for standard nav in light mode (~12.5h to full drain from nav load alone), 5%/hour in dark mode (~20h), and 3%/hour in a new "Power Saving" nav mode (~33h) - all well above the claimed 4-6h "intensive" figure. Separately, the underlying GNSS chip itself draws roughly 30 mA in tracking mode / ~70 mA during acquisition (a small fraction of a typical 3000-5000+ mAh battery's draw budget), and dedicated GNSS chips run at just 4-7 mW in low-power modes - meaning on modern phones the screen and cellular/data radio, not the GPS receiver, are the dominant drain drivers, so the 4-6h "intensive GPS" framing likely conflates full-screen-on turn-by-turn navigation battery use (screen+radio+GPS+CPU rendering) with GPS drain specifically. A product team should budget conservatively (assume large variance: 6-20+ hours depending on screen brightness/mode, device battery size, and whether cellular data is active) rather than treating 8-12h/4-6h as fixed numbers, and should expect real gains from duty-cycling the screen and GNSS independently, since GNSS itself is a comparatively minor power draw versus the display.
- **[CONTESTED]** Continuous aggressive Android BLE scanning (SCAN_MODE_LOW_LATENCY) pulls roughly 30-80 mA on a mid-range device; foreground beacon scanning has been directly measured at ~90 mA, while Android's default adaptive background scan manager cuts this to ~37 mA (~60% savings) by throttling scan windows. _(30-90 mA continuous scan; ~37 mA with default duty-cycling (~60% savings))_
  - The qualitative pattern (aggressive/continuous BLE scan >> duty-cycled background scan, big power savings from throttling) is real and holds up. But the specific numbers in the claim trace to a single lineage of sources, not independent measurements: the 90 mA (foreground) / 37 mA (background, ~60% savings) figures come from the Android Beacon Library's own documentation, which explicitly states these were measured on "the first Nexus 4 device that supported Bluetooth LE" (c.2013 hardware, Bluetooth 4.0) and adds "specifics have certainly changed since then" — i.e. the source itself disclaims current validity, and every other place these numbers appear (dev blogs) is just re-quoting that same page, not a second independent measurement. The 30-80 mA range for SCAN_MODE_LOW_LATENCY on "a mid-range device" comes from a non-peer-reviewed developer/marketing blog post with no disclosed test methodology, device model, or raw data — it reads as a rule-of-thumb estimate, not a benchmark. Independent academic sources I found measure BLE energy at the chipset/protocol level (e.g. ~40 mA/40 mW continuous scan on an nRF51822 SoC, ~5-30 mA typical BLE active-communication range in other technical analyses) which is roughly consistent in order of magnitude but is not a measurement of an actual modern mid-range Android phone's system-level current draw, and doesn't corroborate the specific 30-80 mA or 37 mA figures. Real-world caveats for a product team: (1) treat 30-90 mA and 37 mA as decade-old, single-device anecdata, not a current benchmark — a 2013 Nexus 4 has a very different BT/SoC power profile than 2024+ mid-range silicon (Bluetooth 5.x radios are generally more efficient per unit time); (2) actual draw is highly device/chipset/Android-version dependent and no rigorous multi-device 2023-2026 study was found; (3) the ~60% "default Android background scan manager" savings figure is specifically the Android Beacon Library's own app-level BackgroundPowerSaver behavior (10s scan every 5 min), not necessarily identical to Android OS's built-in system scan throttling behavior, which is a related but distinct mechanism (5 starts/30s throttle, foreground-service exemptions) with its own separate power characteristics; recommend commissioning a fresh Monsoon-power-monitor style measurement on 2-3 current mid-range Android devices before using any of these mA figures in a shippable power budget.
- **[CONTESTED]** In Android's SCAN_MODE_LOW_POWER, the radio scans for only 0.5 s then sleeps 4.5 s (a ~10% duty cycle), which is the mechanism behind most of the large power savings versus continuous low-latency scanning; after ~30 minutes of continuous foreground scanning without a foreground service, Android auto-downgrades an app to SCAN_MODE_OPPORTUNISTIC regardless of requested mode. _(10% duty cycle (0.5s/5s) in low-power scan mode)_
  - The core figure holds up: multiple independent sources (Martijn van Welie's widely-cited "Making Android BLE work" series, and Nordic Semiconductor's official Android-Scanner-Compat-Library README) independently state SCAN_MODE_LOW_POWER scans for 0.5s then sleeps 4.5s, i.e. a 10% duty cycle (500ms/5000ms), and this is indeed the documented mechanism behind BLE background-scan power savings vs SCAN_MODE_LOW_LATENCY (which runs at "highest duty cycle" continuously). The 30-minute auto-downgrade to SCAN_MODE_OPPORTUNISTIC is also well corroborated across independent sources (van Welie's blog, multiple GitHub issues on AltBeacon/android-beacon-library, cordova-plugin-ble-central, and Nordic's compat library issue tracker, plus a directly cited logcat line "clientIf set to scan opportunisticly" firing at ~30min) — this is an OS/Bluetooth-stack-level cap introduced in Android 7, independent of requested scan mode. HOWEVER, the claim's specific qualifier "without a foreground service" is not supported and is likely wrong: in the Nordic Android-Scanner-Compat-Library issue #101, a developer running a persistent foreground service still hit the same 30-minute downgrade, and no source found ties foreground-service status to exemption from this specific BLE-stack timeout (foreground services exempt apps from Doze/App-Standby background execution limits, a different mechanism, not this BLE opportunistic-scan cap). Product teams should treat the 30-min opportunistic downgrade as unconditional and plan to stop/restart scans periodically (well under 30 min) regardless of foreground-service status, and should also beware that Android throttles scan start/stop more than ~5 times per 30 seconds, and that quoted continuous-BLE-scan current draw values (SCAN_MODE_LOW_LATENCY at roughly 30-80mA on mid-range hardware) vary substantially by chipset/OS version and were not independently cross-validated against manufacturer datasheets in this pass.
- **[CONTESTED]** Active camera use (viewfinder/preview: sensor + ISP + display) draws roughly 1000 mW by itself, with video recording (encode) adding another 200-1000 mW on top; this makes continuous camera-on operation one of the single largest power draws on a phone, comparable to or exceeding the application processor under load. _(~1000 mW viewfinder; +200-1000 mW recording)_
  - The specific figures in the claim (~1000 mW viewfinder, +200-1000 mW for recording) trace to a single ultimate source: the Cambridge University Press book "Smartphone Energy Consumption" (Perrucci, Fitzek, Widmer, ~2014), as summarized in Pete Warden's 2015 blog post. Every site repeating these exact numbers (battery-tips blogs, Medium posts, Qnovo) appears to be re-quoting that one blog post, not an independent measurement — so the precise figures are NOT independently corroborated by multiple distinct measurement sources, and the underlying hardware (era of ARM Cortex-A9 SoCs) is roughly a decade out of date for 2026 phones. Modern flagship SoCs, by contrast, can draw well over 3-5 W under sustained heavy compute load (sustained multi-core + GPU), so the claim that camera draw is "comparable to or exceeding the application processor under load" is true only for light/idle-ish AP states, not for a SoC pushed hard (e.g., running real-time on-device CV inference concurrently with the camera, which is exactly the app's use case) — in that concurrent scenario the AP will likely dominate, not the ISP/sensor path alone. A product team should treat 1000-2000 mW as a rough, dated order-of-magnitude planning figure for camera-only draw, budget separately and additively for on-device CV inference on the AP/NPU (which can itself be 500 mW-multiple watts depending on model), and get an actual measured baseline (Monsoon power monitor or equivalent) on the specific target phones before finalizing a duty-cycling strategy, since no current, independently-replicated mW benchmark for 2024-2026 devices was found in this search.
